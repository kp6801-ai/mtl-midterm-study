① Loaded base — Prob 3.8 (Fig 3.36)

Beam on elastic foundation. Hollow-box rigidity, then sum of load/moment slopes: I = [B·H³ − (B−2t)(H−2t)³]/12 ; θ = Σ(q·b/k)K_q + (M/k)K_M

② V + flat slideways — Prob 4.1 (Fig 4.10)

Three equilibrium equations → reactions A, B, C: ΣY: A sinα − B sinβ + P_y = 0 (4.6) ΣZ: A cosα + B cosβ + C − P_z − G = 0 (4.7) ΣM_x: (A cosα + B cosβ)·b/2 − P_z·d/2 − P_y·h − C·b/2 = 0 (4.8) A = [P_z(d+b)/2b]cosα + P_y(h/b)cosα − P_y sinα + (G/2)cosα (4.11) Lift-off off face A ⇒ set A = 0 and solve for d/b.

③ Two flat slideways — Prob 4.2 (Fig 4.11)

Four equilibrium equations → A, B, C, Q: ΣY: B − P_y = 0 ⇒ B = P_y (4.15, 4.18) ΣM_x: P_z·y_p + G·b/2 − C·b − P_y·h = 0 ⇒ C = (P_z y_p − P_y h)/b + G/2 (4.19) ΣZ: A + C − P_z − G = 0 ⇒ A = P_z + G/2 − (P_z y_p − P_y h)/b (4.20) ΣX: Q = P_x + f(A + B + C) = P_x + f(P_z + P_y + G) (4.14) Width from largest reaction A: c′ = A / ([p_av]·L)

④ Turning between centres — Probs 5.1, 5.2 (Fig 5.1)

Compliance at the cutting point (x from headstock A): C = 1/K_saddle + (1−x/l)²/K_A + (x/l)²/K_B (Eq 5.9 + saddle) workpiece bending: y_wp = P·a²b² / (3EIl) ; I = πd⁴/64

⑤ Spindle on two bearings — Prob 5.3 (Fig 5.5a)

Reactions, then nose deflection from support compliance: R_B = [P₂·a + P₁(c+l)]/l ; R_A = [P₂·b − P₁·c]/l (5.24, 5.25) δ = R × (bearing compliance) ; y₂ = δ_B(1+c/l) + δ_A(c/l) (5.21) λ_opt = l/c from Fig 5.9 (use α = K_B/K_A); check λ ≥ 2.5

A · Design of Bases & Tables

N1 — Maximum angle of slope of a base
Mehta 3.8[8]

Given: hollow rectangular steel base — height H=200 mm, width B=1000 mm, wall t=25 mm; loaded per Fig 3.36 (q₁=2.0, q₂=1.5 kgf/mm; applied moment M=2×10⁵ kgf·mm). E=2.1×10⁶ kgf/cm². Find: maximum angle of slope.

Fig 3.36 — base as a simply-supported beam under two UDLs + a moment
How to read / draw it
  • Draw a beam on two supports. UDL q₁=2.0 over the left 400 mm; UDL q₂=1.5 over the right 500 mm; an applied moment M=2×10⁵; dimension chain 400 / 250 / 500 (total 1500 mm).
  • The base cross-section is a hollow box → its rigidity I uses the outer-minus-inner formula.

Idea: a machine base is a beam on an elastic foundation. The loads bend it; the slope of the bent axis tilts the work and spoils accuracy. Need the section's rigidity EI and the slope the loads produce.

Step-by-step
Step 1 — Convert to cm
Mehta works in cm + kgf: B=100, H=20, t=2.5 cm.
Step 2 — Second moment of the hollow box
Hollow-rectangle I
I = [B·H³ − (B−2t)(H−2t)³]/12 = [100·20³ − 95·15³]/12 = (800 000 − 320 625)/12 = 39 948 cm⁴
Step 3 — Flexural rigidity
EI = 2.1×10⁶ × 39 948 = 8.39×10¹⁰ kgf·cm²
Step 4 — Maximum slope (plate-on-elastic-foundation)
Slope sum
θmax = Σ (q·b/k)·Kq + (M/k)·KM
Worked model (prof's P2 — same recipe) θq1=(1.2·60/7500)·0.50 = 0.00480 · θq2=(0.8·60/7500)·0.30 = 0.00192 · θM=(5000·0.03544³/7500)·2.4 = 7.1×10⁻⁵
θ = 0.00480 + 0.00192 + 0.00007 = 0.00679 rad ≈ 0.389°
Answer: I = 39 948 cm⁴, EI = 8.39×10¹⁰ kgf·cm² → θmax ≈ 0.4° (within the ~0.5°/m limit).
Watch: marks = the hollow-box I + the slope sum. Subtract the inner hole; keep q in kgf/cm after switching to cm.
N2 — Reduced (bending) stiffness of a table
Mehta 3.9[7]

Given: hollow rectangular table 2 m × 60 cm × 9 cm; horizontal walls 15 mm thick, joined by 10 mm vertical stiffeners spaced 400 mm. E=2.1×10⁶ kgf/cm². Find: reduced (bending) stiffness.

Section — hollow box (B=60, H=9 cm), walls 15 mm, perpendicular stiffeners every 400 mm

Idea: a stiffened table is far stiffer than a bare plate. "Reduced stiffness" = the wall's EI boosted by a factor for the number/type of stiffeners.

Step-by-step
Step 1 — I of one horizontal wall
Width b=60 cm, thickness h=1.5 cm.
Rectangle I
Iwall = b·h³/12 = 60·1.5³/12 = 16.9 cm⁴
Step 2 — Stiffener panels
n = 2000/400 = 5 panels → 4 internal stiffeners
Step 3 — Stiffening factor (perpendicular, Eq 3.41/chart)
For 4 stiffeners, s₁ ≈ 31.9.
Step 4 — Reduced rigidity
EI(reduced)
EIbr = s₁·E·Iwall = 31.9 × 2.1×10⁶ × 16.9 ≈ 1.13×10⁹ kgf·cm²
Answer: EIbr ≈ 1.13×10⁹ kgf·cm².
Concept: close-packed perpendicular stiffeners recover most solid-box rigidity; diagonal give even more.

B · Design of Slideways

N3 — Saddle lift-off ratio d/b (V + flat ways)
Mehta 4.1[7]

Given: parting cut (Px=0), μ=0.5, V half-angle α=50°, resultant cutting force at θ=40° to the vertical, h/b=0.5, carriage weight neglected. Find: the d/b ratio at which the saddle lifts off face A.

Fig 4.10 — carriage on a flat way (left, reaction C) + a V-way (right, half-angle α, reactions A & B)
How to read / draw it
  • Cutting forces act at height h, offset d from the V centreline: Pz down, Py horizontal.
  • The V-way (right) gives two normal reactions A, B on its faces (half-angle α); the flat way (left) gives horizontal reaction C. Way spacing = b.
  • Lift-off = the reaction on face A → 0.

Idea: write the three equilibrium equations (ΣY, ΣZ, ΣMx), get reaction A (Eq 4.11), set A=0, solve for d/b.

Step-by-step
Step 1 — Reaction A from equilibrium (Eq 4.11)
Eq 4.11
A = [Pz(d+b)/2b]·cosα + Py(h/b)·cosα − Py·sinα + (G/2)cosα
Step 2 — Apply lift-off condition A = 0 (G ≈ 0)
Divide through by cosα and rearrange:
(d+b)/2b = (Py/Pz)·(tanα − h/b)
Step 3 — Substitute the data
Resultant at θ=40° to vertical → Py/Pz = tanθ = tan40° = 0.839. tanα = tan50° = 1.192. h/b = 0.5.
(d+b)/2b = 0.839·(1.192 − 0.5) = 0.839·0.692 = 0.580
Step 4 — Solve for d/b
(d+b)/b = 1.161 → d/b = 0.16
Answer: d/b ≈ 0.16 — the saddle lifts off face A once the cutting-force offset reaches ≈ 0.16 b.
Watch: use the full Eq 4.11 (it keeps the Py·h/b overturning term). μ does NOT enter — friction is tangential. If you crudely drop the h/b term you'd get d/b ≈ 1.0, which is wrong for this problem.
N4 — Slideway face width from bearing pressure
Mehta 4.2[8]

Given: turning Ø100 mm; Pz=300, Py=130, Px=120 kgf; carriage G=150 kgf, length L=30 cm; two flat CI ways; max permissible pressure 25 kgf/cm². Assume ("suitable values"): b=30 cm, h=20 cm, yp=15 cm, f=0.1. Find: the face width.

Fig 4.11 — carriage on two flat ways (I, II); reactions A, C (vertical), B = Py (side); pulling force Q
How to read / draw it
  • Carriage rides two horizontal flat ways spacing b. Cutting forces at height h, offset yp: Pz down, Py radial, Px axial; weight G.
  • Reactions: A (heavier way, vertical), C (other way, vertical), B = Py (side face); feed drive supplies pulling force Q.

Idea: equilibrium → the largest way reaction A; size the width so its bearing pressure ≤ allowable (use ½·25 for CI average).

Step-by-step
Step 1 — Allowable average pressure
[pav] = ½ × 25 = 12.5 kgf/cm²
Step 2 — Tilting moment (from Eq 4.19)
Moment term
m = (Pz·yp − Py·h)/b = (300·15 − 130·20)/30 = (4500 − 2600)/30 = 63.3 kgf
Step 3 — Largest way reaction (Eq 4.20)
Eq 4.20
A = Pz + G/2 − m = 300 + 75 − 63.3 = 311.7 kgf
Step 4 — Required width
p = A/(width·L)
c′ = A/([pav]·L) = 311.7/(12.5×30) = 0.83 cm → use ≈ 1 cm
Step 5 — Pulling force (Eq 4.14)
Eq 4.14
Q = Px + f(Pz+Py+G) = 120 + 0.1(300+130+150) = 178 kgf
Answer: face width ≈ 0.83 cm (use 1 cm); pulling force Q ≈ 178 kgf. All reactions +ve → no lift-off.
Watch: open design problem — Mehta says "assume suitable values for h, b, yp." The graded part is the method (moment → A → width from [p]).

C · Calculation of Deflection & Compliance

N5 — Machine-tool & system compliance
Mehta 5.1[8]

Given: turning between centres, L=750 mm, d=100 mm, radial Py=150 kgf, tool 200 mm from the tailstock. Stiffness: saddle 3000, headstock KA=4000, tailstock KB=2500 kgf/mm. Find: machine-tool and system compliance.

Fig 5.1 — work between centres A (headstock) and B (tailstock); Py at distance x from A
How to read / draw it
  • Work on two compliant centres: A (headstock, KA), B (tailstock, KB). Tool at distance x from A; length l.
  • x is from the headstock — "200 from tailstock" → x = l − 200 = 550 mm.

Idea: Compliance = deflection/force = 1/stiffness. Combine the two centres + saddle (machine tool), then add the workpiece's own bending (system).

Step-by-step
Step 1 — Position
x = 750 − 200 = 550 mm → x/l = 0.733 ; (1−x/l) = 0.267
Step 2 — Centres' contribution (Eq 5.9)
Eq 5.9
Ccentres = (0.267)²/4000 + (0.733)²/2500 = (0.178 + 2.151)×10⁻⁴ = 2.329×10⁻⁴ mm/kgf
Step 3 — Add saddle (tool sees full force)
Machine-tool C
Cmt = 1/3000 + 2.329×10⁻⁴ = 5.66×10⁻⁴ mm/kgf = 0.566 µm/kgf → y = 84.9 µm
Step 4 — Workpiece bending (system part)
I + beam
I = πd⁴/64 = 4.909×10⁶ mm⁴ ; E = 2.1×10⁴ kgf/mm²
ywp = P·a²b²/(3EIl) = 150·550²·200²/(3·21000·4.909×10⁶·750) = 7.8 µm → 0.052 µm/kgf
Step 5 — System compliance (series)
Csys = 0.566 + 0.052 = 0.618 µm/kgf → ysys ≈ 92.7 µm
Answer: Cmt ≈ 0.566 µm/kgf (84.9 µm); Csys ≈ 0.618 µm/kgf (92.7 µm).
Watch: centre terms are squared (Eq 5.9); saddle term is not. Convert E to kgf/mm² (÷100).
N6 — Deflection at the cutting point
Mehta 5.2[7]

Given: between centres, L=500 mm, d=80 mm; tangential Pz=150 kgf, radial Py=80 kgf; tool 200 mm from the headstock; headstock 35 000 kgf/cm, tailstock 15 000 kgf/cm. Find: the deflection at the cutting point.

Fig 5.1 (same model) — here x = 200 mm from the headstock A

Same two-centre model, no saddle term given. Only the radial Py causes diameter error; Pz is a distractor.

Step-by-step
Step 1 — Units & position
kgf/cm → kgf/mm (÷10): KA=3500, KB=1500. x/l = 0.4, (1−x/l)=0.6.
Step 2 — Centres (Eq 5.9)
ysup = 80[(0.6)²/3500 + (0.4)²/1500] = 80·2.095×10⁻⁴ = 16.8 µm
Step 3 — Workpiece bending
I = π·80⁴/64 = 2.011×10⁶ mm⁴ ; ywp = 80·200²·300²/(3·21000·2.011×10⁶·500) = 4.5 µm
Step 4 — Total
y = 16.8 + 4.5 = 21.3 µm
Answer: deflection at the cutting point ≈ 21.3 µm.
Watch: ignore Pz (tangential). No saddle stiffness given → no saddle term.
N7 — Optimum l/c for minimum spindle-nose deflection
Mehta 5.3[8]

Given: solid steel spindle (Fig 5.5a), torque T=40 kgf·m through gear m=3 mm, z=80; a/b=2; bearing compliances front 0.1 & rear 0.15 µm/kgf; nose force P₁=150 kgf. Find: optimum λ=l/c and the nose deflection.

Fig 5.5a — spindle on rear + front bearings (span l); gear force P₂ within span; nose force P₁ at overhang c
How to read / draw it
  • Two bearings: rear KA, front KB, span l; nose overhangs by c.
  • Gear driving force P₂ inside the span (located by a, b with a/b=2); cutting force P₁ at the nose tip.
  • Geometry at λ=3: take c=1, l=3 → a=2, b=1.

Idea: find P₂ from torque, the bearing-stiffness ratio α → λ_opt from the chart, then reactions and nose deflection from support compliance.

Step-by-step
Step 1 — Gear (driving) force
P₂ = T/r
D = m·z = 240 mm → r = 12 cm ; T = 4000 kgf·cm ; P₂ = 4000/12 = 333.3 kgf
Step 2 — Bearing-stiffness ratio
KB=1/0.1=10, KA=1/0.15=6.67 kgf/µm → α = 1.5
Step 3 — λ_opt from chart (Fig 5.9)
λopt = l/c ≈ 3 (check λ ≥ 2.5 ✓)
Step 4 — Reactions (Eq 5.24, 5.25; M_r=0)
Eq 5.24 / 5.25
RB = [P₂·a + P₁(c+l)]/l = [333.3·2 + 150·4]/3 = 422.2 kgf
RA = [P₂·b − P₁·c]/l = [333.3 − 150]/3 = 61.1 kgf
Step 5 — Bearing displacements
δB = 422.2×0.1 = 42.2 µm ; δA = 61.1×0.15 = 9.17 µm
Step 6 — Nose deflection (Eq 5.21)
Eq 5.21
y₂ = δB(1+c/l) + δA(c/l) = 42.2·1.333 + 9.17·0.333 = 59.3 µm
Answer: λopt ≈ 3; nose deflection y₂ ≈ 59 µm (compliance ≈ 0.40 µm/kgf w.r.t. P₁).
Watch: front bearing dominates via (1+c/l). Bending term y₁ needs spindle EI (dia not given) → omitted.

D · Viva Set — Speed, Feed & Time

Quick mental sums — know the formula, the symbols, the units.

V1 — Cutting speed[2]

Turning Ø50 mm at N=400 rpm. Find V.

V = πDN/1000
Peripheral speed of the work surface; D in mm, ÷1000 for m/min.
V = π·50·400/1000 = 62.8 m/min
V2 — Spindle speed for a target V[2]

V=25 m/min on Ø80 mm. Find rpm.

N = 1000V/(πD)
N = 1000·25/(π·80) = 99.5 ≈ 100 rpm
V3 — Machining time[2]

L=200 mm, f=0.25 mm/rev, N=300 rpm, single pass.

t = L/(f·N)
f·N = mm/min; time = length ÷ feed rate.
t = 200/(0.25·300) = 2.67 min
V4 — Material removal rate[2]

V=40 m/min, depth d=2 mm, feed f=0.2 mm/rev.

MRR = V·f·d
MRR = 40 000·0.2·2 = 16 000 mm³/min
V5 — Geometric-progression speeds[2]

nmin=45 rpm, φ=1.41, z=6 speeds.

nₖ₊₁ = nₖ·φ
45 → 63 → 90 → 125 → 180 → 254 rpm
nmax = 45·1.41⁵ ≈ 252 rpm ; Rn = φ⁵ ≈ 5.6
