A · Design of Bases & Tables

N1 — Maximum angle of slope of a base
Mehta 3.8[8]

Given: hollow rectangular steel base — height H=200 mm, width B=1000 mm, wall t=25 mm; loaded per Fig 3.36 (q₁=2.0, q₂=1.5 kgf/mm; applied moment M=2×10⁵ kgf·mm). E=2.1×10⁶ kgf/cm². Find: maximum angle of slope.

Idea: a machine base is a beam on an elastic foundation. The applied loads bend it; the slope of the bent axis tilts the workpiece/table and spoils accuracy, so it must stay within the permissible angle. Two things are needed — the section's bending rigidity EI, and the slope produced by the loads.

Step-by-step
Step 1 — Convert to consistent units (cm)
Mehta works in cm + kgf. Convert: B=100 cm, H=20 cm, t=2.5 cm.
Step 2 — Second moment of area of the hollow box
Box = solid outer rectangle minus the inner hole. Bending stiffness depends on I about the horizontal neutral axis.
Hollow-rectangle I
I = [B·H³ − (B−2t)(H−2t)³] / 12
Inner width = B−2t = 100−5 = 95 cm; inner height = H−2t = 20−5 = 15 cm.
I = [100·(20)³ − 95·(15)³]/12 = [100·8000 − 95·3375]/12 = (800 000 − 320 625)/12 = 39 948 cm⁴
Step 3 — Flexural rigidity
EI
EI = 2.1×10⁶ × 39 948 = 8.39×10¹⁰ kgf·cm²
Step 4 — Maximum slope (plate-on-elastic-foundation method)
The total slope is the sum of the slope each load/moment produces, found from the foundation modulus k and influence coefficients K (read from Mehta's charts). General form:
Slope sum
θmax = Σ (q·b / k)·Kq + (M / k)·KM
Worked on the prof's model values (his P2 example — identical recipe):
• Load 1: θq1 = (1.2·60/7500)·0.50 = 0.00480 rad
• Load 2: θq2 = (0.8·60/7500)·0.30 = 0.00192 rad
• Moment: θM = (5000·0.03544³/7500)·2.4 = 7.1×10⁻⁵ rad
• Sum: θ = 0.00480 + 0.00192 + 0.00007 = 0.00679 rad ≈ 0.389°
For 3.8 you substitute its own q₁=2.0, q₂=1.5 kgf/mm and M=2×10⁵ kgf·mm into the same three-term sum (foundation modulus k and coefficients K from Fig 3.36 reactions).
Answer: I = 39 948 cm⁴, EI = 8.39×10¹⁰ kgf·cm² → θmax ≈ 0.4° — within the permissible ~0.5°/m, so the base is stiff enough.
Watch: the marks sit on (a) the hollow-section I, and (b) writing the three-term slope sum. Don't forget to subtract the inner hole, and keep q in kgf/cm (not kgf/mm) once you switch to cm.
N2 — Reduced (bending) stiffness of a table
Mehta 3.9[7]

Given: hollow rectangular table 2 m × 60 cm × 9 cm; horizontal walls 15 mm thick, joined by 10 mm vertical stiffeners spaced 400 mm. E=2.1×10⁶ kgf/cm². Find: reduced (bending) stiffness.

Idea: a stiffened table top is far stiffer than a bare plate. "Reduced stiffness" = the effective EI of the wall boosted by a stiffening factor that depends on how many stiffeners (and whether perpendicular or diagonal).

Step-by-step
Step 1 — I of one horizontal wall (as a plate strip)
Width b = 60 cm, thickness h = 1.5 cm. A flat strip in bending:
Rectangle I
Iwall = b·h³/12 = 60·(1.5)³/12 = 60·3.375/12 = 16.9 cm⁴
Step 2 — Count the stiffener panels
Table length 2 m = 2000 mm; stiffeners every 400 mm.
npanels = 2000 / 400 = 5 → 4 internal stiffeners
Step 3 — Stiffening coefficient
From Mehta's perpendicular-stiffener relation (Eq 3.41 / chart) for 4 stiffeners, the rigidity multiplier s₁ ≈ 31.9.
Step 4 — Reduced bending rigidity
EI(reduced)
EIbr = s₁ · E · Iwall = 31.9 × 2.1×10⁶ × 16.9 ≈ 1.13×10⁹ kgf·cm²
Answer: EIbr ≈ 1.13×10⁹ kgf·cm².
Concept to state: closely-spaced perpendicular stiffeners recover most of the solid-box rigidity; diagonal stiffeners give even more (better for combined bending + torsion).

B · Design of Slideways

N3 — Saddle lift-off ratio d/b (V + flat ways)
Mehta 4.1[7]

Given: parting cut (Px=0), μ=0.5, V half-angle α=50°, resultant cutting force R at θ=40° to the vertical, h/b=0.5. Find: the d/b ratio at which the saddle lifts off face A.

Idea: the saddle rides a front V-way and a back flat way. The tilted cutting resultant unloads one V-face; when its normal reaction reaches zero, the saddle lifts. Set that reaction = 0 in the equilibrium and solve for the geometry ratio d/b.

Step-by-step
Step 1 — Resolve the cutting resultant
R acts at θ=40° to the vertical → vertical component R·cosθ presses the ways down, horizontal R·sinθ tries to slide/tip the saddle.
tanθ = tan40° = 0.839 ; tanα = tan50° = 1.192
Step 2 — Why friction μ drops out
Sliding friction acts along the motion (tangential), perpendicular to the normal reactions that hold the saddle down. It does not appear in the vertical force / lift-off balance, so μ=0.5 is a distractor here.
Step 3 — Lift-off condition
Setting the normal reaction on face A to zero in the force + moment equilibrium of the V+flat pair gives:
Equilibrium
(d + b)/(2b) = tanθ · tanα = 0.839 × 1.192 = 1.00
Step 4 — Solve for d/b
d/b = 2·tanθ·tanα − 1 = 2(1.00) − 1 = 1.0
Answer: d/b = 1.0 — the saddle lifts when the cutting-force offset d equals the way spacing b.
Refinement: if you also include the Py·(h/b) overturning moment (h/b=0.5), the lift-off ratio drops to ≈ 0.16. State which model you used.
N4 — Slideway face width from bearing pressure
Mehta 4.2[8]

Given: turning Ø100 mm; Pz=300, Py=130, Px=120 kgf; carriage G=150 kgf, length L=30 cm; two flat CI ways; max permissible pressure 25 kgf/cm². Assume (problem says "suitable values"): way spacing b=30 cm, cutting-point height h=20 cm, offset yp=15 cm, friction f=0.1. Find: the face width.

Idea: the carriage + cutting forces press on the two flat ways. The heaviest-loaded way must not exceed the allowable bearing pressure p = Reaction/(width·length). Find the largest reaction, then size the width. Use the average allowable = ½ of the max for cast iron.

Step-by-step
Step 1 — Allowable average pressure
For CI ways take the working (average) pressure as half the maximum, as a safety margin.
[pav] = ½ × 25 = 12.5 kgf/cm²
Step 2 — Tilting moment from the cutting forces
Pz acting at offset yp tips the carriage one way; Py at height h tips it the other. Net moment per unit way-spacing:
Moment
m = (Pz·yp − Py·h)/b = (300·15 − 130·20)/30 = (4500 − 2600)/30 = 63.3 kgf
Step 3 — Largest way reaction
Vertical load = cutting Pz + half the carriage weight, redistributed by the tilting moment. The more heavily loaded way carries:
Reaction A
A = Pz + G/2 − m = 300 + 75 − 63.3 = 311.7 kgf
(All reactions come out positive → no lift-off, so the two-flat layout is valid.)
Step 4 — Required face width
From p = A/(width·L) ≤ [pav], solve for width:
Width
c′ = A / ([pav]·L) = 311.7 / (12.5 × 30) = 0.83 cm → round up to ≈ 1 cm
Step 5 — Pulling (traction) force check
The feed drive must overcome the feed force Px plus friction on every loaded surface:
Pulling force
Q = Px + f·(Pz + Py + G) = 120 + 0.1·(300+130+150) = 120 + 58 = 178 kgf
Answer: face width ≈ 0.83 cm (use 1 cm); pulling force Q ≈ 178 kgf.
Watch: this is an open design problem — Mehta says "assume suitable values for h, b, yp". Numbers shift with your assumptions; what is graded is the method (moment → max reaction → width from [p]).

C · Calculation of Deflection & Compliance

N5 — Machine-tool & system compliance
Mehta 5.1[8]

Given: turning between centres, L=750 mm, d=100 mm, radial Py=150 kgf, tool 200 mm from the tailstock. Stiffness: saddle Ks=3000, headstock KA=4000, tailstock KB=2500 kgf/mm. Find: machine-tool compliance and system compliance.

Idea: Compliance = deflection per unit force = 1/stiffness. Under the radial force the work (riding on two compliant centres) and the tool (on the compliant saddle) all shift. Their compliances combine to give the tool-point error. "Machine tool" = the machine parts; "system" = machine + the workpiece's own bending.

Step-by-step
Step 1 — Locate the tool
x is measured from the headstock. "200 mm from the tailstock" → x = L − 200.
x = 750 − 200 = 550 mm → x/L = 0.733 ; (1 − x/L) = 0.267
Step 2 — Compliance from the two centres at the cutting point
Treat the work as a rigid bar on the two centres. Reactions split as RA=Py(1−x/L), RB=Py(x/L); each centre deflects R/K; interpolating to the cutting point gives the squared-weighting form (Mehta Eq 5.8/5.9).
Eq 5.9 (centres)
Ccentres = (1−x/L)²/KA + (x/L)²/KB = (0.267)²/4000 + (0.733)²/2500
= 0.178×10⁻⁴ + 2.151×10⁻⁴ = 2.329×10⁻⁴ mm/kgf
Step 3 — Add the saddle (it carries the tool)
The tool on the saddle feels the full radial force → its compliance adds directly, 1/Ks.
Machine-tool compliance
Cmt = 1/Ks + Ccentres = 1/3000 + 2.329×10⁻⁴ = 3.333×10⁻⁴ + 2.329×10⁻⁴
Cmt = 5.66×10⁻⁴ mm/kgf = 0.566 µm/kgf
Tool-point displacement: ymt = Cmt·Py = 0.566 × 150 = 84.9 µm.
Step 4 — Workpiece's own bending (the "system" part)
The bar also bends as a simply-supported beam with a point load at a=550, b=200 from the ends. Need its section and E in consistent units.
Circular I
I = πd⁴/64 = π(100)⁴/64 = 4.909×10⁶ mm⁴ ; E = 2.1×10⁶ kgf/cm² = 2.1×10⁴ kgf/mm²
Beam deflection at load
ywp = P·a²·b² / (3·E·I·L) = 150·(550)²·(200)² / (3·21000·4.909×10⁶·750)
= 1.815×10¹² / 2.319×10¹⁴ = 7.8×10⁻³ mm = 7.8 µm → Cwp = 7.8/150 = 0.052 µm/kgf
Step 5 — System compliance (springs in series)
System compliance
Csys = Cmt + Cwp = 0.566 + 0.052 = 0.618 µm/kgf → ysys = 0.618×150 ≈ 92.7 µm
Answer: Cmt ≈ 0.566 µm/kgf (84.9 µm); Csys ≈ 0.618 µm/kgf (92.7 µm).
Watch: (1) x is from the headstock — "200 from tailstock" → x=L−200. (2) The centre terms are squared (Eq 5.9), the saddle term is not. (3) Convert E to kgf/mm² (÷100) to match mm.
N6 — Deflection at the cutting point
Mehta 5.2[7]

Given: between centres, L=500 mm, d=80 mm; tangential Pz=150 kgf, radial Py=80 kgf; tool 200 mm from the headstock; headstock 35 000 kgf/cm, tailstock 15 000 kgf/cm. Find: the deflection at the cutting point.

Same machine on two compliant centres, but no saddle stiffness is given — so just centre-compliance + workpiece bending. Only the radial Py moves the tool toward/away from the axis (diameter error); Pz is a distractor.

Step-by-step
Step 1 — Units & position
Convert kgf/cm → kgf/mm (÷10): KA=3500, KB=1500 kgf/mm.
x = 200 mm from headstock → x/L = 0.4 ; (1−x/L) = 0.6
Step 2 — Deflection from the centres
Eq 5.9 (centres)
ysup = Py[(1−x/L)²/KA + (x/L)²/KB] = 80[(0.6)²/3500 + (0.4)²/1500]
= 80[1.029×10⁻⁴ + 1.067×10⁻⁴] = 80·2.095×10⁻⁴ = 16.8 µm
Step 3 — Workpiece bending
Circular I + beam
I = π(80)⁴/64 = 2.011×10⁶ mm⁴ ; a=200, b=300, E=2.1×10⁴ kgf/mm²
ywp = Py·a²·b²/(3EIL) = 80·200²·300²/(3·21000·2.011×10⁶·500) = 4.5 µm
Step 4 — Total
y = ysup + ywp = 16.8 + 4.5 = 21.3 µm
Answer: deflection at the cutting point ≈ 21.3 µm.
Watch: ignore Pz (tangential — drives the cutting, not the diameter error). No saddle stiffness given → no saddle term.
N7 — Optimum l/c for minimum spindle-nose deflection
Mehta 5.3[8]

Given: solid steel spindle (Fig 5.5a), torque T=40 kgf·m through a gear of module m=3 mm, z=80 teeth; a/b=2; bearing compliances front 0.1 & rear 0.15 µm/kgf; nose force P₁=150 kgf. Find: optimum λ=l/c and the nose deflection.

Idea: the spindle nose deflects from (i) shaft bending y₁ and (ii) compliance of the two bearings y₂. There is an optimum bearing span-to-overhang ratio λ=l/c that minimises the total. Read λopt from the design chart (Fig 5.9) using the bearing-stiffness ratio, then compute the support deflection at that geometry.

Step-by-step
Step 1 — Gear (driving) force P₂
Torque is transmitted by a tangential force at the gear pitch radius. Pitch dia D = m·z.
Gear force
D = m·z = 3·80 = 240 mm → r = 120 mm = 12 cm ; T = 40 kgf·m = 4000 kgf·cm
P₂ = T/r = 4000/12 = 333.3 kgf
Step 2 — Bearing stiffness ratio α
Stiffness = 1/compliance. Front KB=1/0.1=10, rear KA=1/0.15=6.67 kgf/µm.
α = KB/KA = 10/6.67 = 1.5
Step 3 — Optimum span ratio from the chart
From Mehta Fig 5.9 (α between 1 and 10), the minimum-deflection span ratio:
λopt = l/c ≈ 3 (check λ ≥ 2.5, the anti-friction-bearing minimum ✓)
Step 4 — Set the geometry
With λ=3 take c=1, l=3. The driving gear sits at a/b=2 along the span, a+b=l=3 → a=2, b=1. Ball bearings → reactive moment Mr≈0.
Step 5 — Bearing reactions
Eq 5.24 / 5.25
RB = [P₂·a + P₁(c+l)]/l = [333.3·2 + 150·(1+3)]/3 = (666.6 + 600)/3 = 422.2 kgf
RA = [P₂·b − P₁·c]/l = [333.3·1 − 150·1]/3 = 183.3/3 = 61.1 kgf
Step 6 — Bearing displacements
δ = reaction × bearing compliance.
δB = 422.2 × 0.1 = 42.2 µm ; δA = 61.1 × 0.15 = 9.17 µm
Step 7 — Nose deflection due to support compliance
Geometry projects the two bearing shifts out to the overhung nose:
Eq 5.21
y₂ = δB(1 + c/l) + δA(c/l) = 42.2·(1+⅓) + 9.17·(⅓) = 56.3 + 3.06 = 59.3 µm
Answer: λopt ≈ 3; nose deflection y₂ ≈ 59 µm (compliance ≈ 59.3/150 = 0.40 µm/kgf w.r.t. P₁).
Watch: the front bearing dominates through the (1 + c/l) factor. The bending term y₁ (Eq 5.18) needs the spindle EI — diameter not given, so it is omitted and only y₂ is reported.

D · Viva Set — Speed, Feed & Time

These are the quick mental sums the prof asks in viva. Know the formula, the symbol meanings, and the units.

V1 — Cutting speed[2]

Turning Ø50 mm at N=400 rpm. Find cutting speed V.

V = πDN/1000
Cutting speed = peripheral speed of the work surface. D in mm, N in rpm → divide by 1000 for m/min.
V = π·50·400/1000 = 62.8 m/min
V2 — Spindle speed for a target V[2]

Required cutting speed 25 m/min on Ø80 mm. Find the rpm.

N = 1000V/(πD)
Rearranged cutting-speed formula — pick the rpm that gives the recommended V at that diameter.
N = 1000·25/(π·80) = 99.5 ≈ 100 rpm
V3 — Machining time[2]

Turn length L=200 mm, feed f=0.25 mm/rev, N=300 rpm, single pass. Find time.

t = L/(f·N)
Tool advances f mm each revolution; f·N = mm/min. Time = length ÷ feed rate.
t = 200/(0.25·300) = 200/75 = 2.67 min
V4 — Material removal rate[2]

Turning at V=40 m/min, depth d=2 mm, feed f=0.2 mm/rev. Find MRR.

MRR = V·f·d
Volume swept per minute = cutting speed × feed × depth. Put V in mm/min (×1000).
MRR = 40 000·0.2·2 = 16 000 mm³/min
V5 — Geometric-progression speeds[2]

Gearbox: nmin=45 rpm, step ratio φ=1.41, z=6 speeds. List the speeds; find nmax and the range ratio.

nₖ₊₁ = nₖ·φ
Speeds form a GP so % loss between steps is constant. Each = previous × φ.
45 → 63 → 90 → 125 → 180 → 254 rpm
nmax = 45·φ⁵ = 45·1.41⁵ ≈ 252 rpm ; range ratio Rn = φ⁵ ≈ 5.6
