MODULE 1

Machine Tools, Drives & Kinematics

What a machine tool is & basic elements

A machine tool is a power-driven machine that uses a cutting tool to remove material from a workpiece to give the required shape, size and smooth surface at high precision. It holds a cutting tool and a workpiece in fixed/controlled relative motion and removes excess material layer by layer (metals, plastics, composites — by cutting, drilling, milling, grinding), controlling size, geometry and finish.

Basic elements of any machine tool: drive system (motor, belts, gears) · structure (bed, frame) · tool-holding mechanism · workpiece-holding mechanism · control system.

Types, applications & evolution

Types & use: lathe (shafts, bolts, cylindrical parts), milling (gears, slots, flats), drilling (holes), grinding (high finish/precision), CNC (automated mass production). Used in automotive, aerospace, tool manufacturing, dies/moulds.

  • Early (before 1900): manual; hand/water/steam power; low accuracy, slow.
  • Industrial revolution (1900–50): electric motors, gear/belt drives, stronger structures → higher speed, better finish, repeatability.
  • Modern (after 1950): NC → CNC → automation/robotics → high precision, mass production, complex shapes, less human error.
  • Today: part of Flexible Manufacturing Systems, smart factories, Industry 4.0 (sensors, CNC/PLC, robots, CAD/CAM).
Drive systems

Drive systems supply and control power, speed and precision from motor to tool/work.

  • Belt drive — flexible, cheap, shock-absorbing; but slip + less accurate speed control (older lathes/drills).
  • Gear drive — high torque, no slip, accurate ratios (heavy lathes, milling).
  • Motor/servo (direct) drive — motor coupled to spindle, electronic speed control, high precision, minimal loss (CNC).
  • Individual drive — each machine has its own motor → transmission → spindle (cutting) + feed mechanism. Independent speed, efficient, modern.
  • Group drive — one motor → many machines via a line shaft; economical but obsolete.

Backgear mechanism (gear-driven individual drive): small gear on the belt pulley + bull gear on the spindle + backgear cluster on a pivoting plate. Direct mode = pulley gear drives the spindle directly (high speed, light cuts). Backgear mode = cluster engaged → power through intermediate gears → spindle speed drops, torque rises (heavy cuts). In a lathe, one motor supplies both cutting and feed motions through different mechanisms.

Process kinematics & working motions

Material removal needs relative motion between tool and workpiece — no motion, no cutting.

  • Cutting (primary) motion — highest velocity, removes material, sets cutting speed (work rotation in lathe, cutter rotation in milling, ram reciprocation in shaper).
  • Feed motion — advances the tool into the work, sets MRR, slower than cutting (tool travel in lathe, table travel in milling).
  • Depth-of-cut motion — penetration into material, sets chip thickness (cross-slide in lathe).

Lathe = single-point continuous cutting (one edge, continuous chip); feed longitudinal/cross/angular(taper). Milling = multi-point, intermittent cutting, higher MRR. Drilling = tool rotates + axial feed. Shaper = tool reciprocates, work indexes stepwise.

Kinematic chains
  • Speed chain: motor → gearbox → spindle (generates cutting motion, controls spindle speed).
  • Feed chain: spindle → change gears → feed gearbox → lead screw → tool movement (controlled feed).

Elements: gears, belts, lead screws, gearboxes. The kinematic chain determines speed range, feed range, torque transmission, machine accuracy and vibration behaviour — even a powerful motor gives poor results with a poor chain.

MODULE 2

Regulation of Speed & Feed

Need for regulation

A machine can't work efficiently at one fixed speed: workpiece diameters vary, tool materials differ, cutting conditions change, material properties differ (steel vs aluminium). Single speed → tool breakage, poor finish, overheating.

Speed regulation

Speed regulation = the arrangement giving multiple spindle speeds so machining runs efficiently and safely under different conditions. Different materials and rough-vs-finish cuts need different speeds; tool life and finish depend on the right speed.

  • Slow speed — heavy cuts, large diameters, high torque, roughing.
  • High speed — finishing, small diameters, better finish, lower forces.
  • Stepped vs continuous: stepped = discrete levels, gearbox-based, conventional (e.g. 100/200/400/800 rpm); continuous = smooth, electronic, CNC.
  • Achieved by: step pulleys, gearboxes, backgear, variable-speed drives, CNC spindle control → better tool life, less chatter, better finish, higher efficiency.
Feed regulation

Feed regulation = the arrangement giving different feed rates so the tool moves at the required speed relative to the work. Rough cutting → higher feed; finishing → smaller feed.

  • Low feed → finishing, accuracy, finish. High feed → roughing, fast removal, productivity.
  • Balance feed with cutting speed, depth, tool and work material — wrong feed → vibration, poor finish, tool breakage.

Feed vs speed: feed controls tool movement / affects finish / too high → rough surface; speed controls cutting velocity / affects temperature / too high → tool wear. S/N ratio (Taguchi): roughness "smaller-is-better", MRR "larger-is-better", higher S/N = better. Design is a compromise of speed range, power, torque, cost — high speed → low torque, low speed → high torque.

Stepped speed design (GP)

Key terms: speed range, number of steps Z, step ratio. Speeds follow geometric progression (each speed = previous × the step ratio) — the standard arrangement in the slides; choose Z, then round to standardized values.

Flow, structural & ray diagrams
  • Flow diagram — sequence/order of transmission elements + direction of power flow + possible paths. Does NOT show dimensions, tooth numbers, geometry.
  • Structural diagram — arrangement of speed-changing stages, how the total number of speeds is obtained (shafts, gear groups, complexity). For 8 speeds, 2×2×2 preferred over 4×2 (balanced load); more stages → smoother but more complex.
  • Ray diagram — how speeds arise from gear-pair combinations across stages. Shafts = vertical parallel equally-spaced lines; speeds plotted on a logarithmic scale with spacing = log φ; each ray = a gear engagement between two shafts. Shows stages, options/stage, all paths, speed multiplication — not tooth numbers/casing.
MODULE 3

Machine-Tool Structures & Guideways

Structures — definition & requirements

The structure is the framework supporting all components and holding the tool–work relationship: bed, column, table, saddle, cross-slide, spindle housing, ram, base. (Lathe: bed, headstock, tailstock, carriage, saddle, cross-slide. Milling: base, column, knee, saddle, table.)

  • High static stiffness — cutting force travels a chain (tool→holder→post→carriage→bed→foundation); each part has its own stiffness → the machine behaves like springs in series.
  • High dynamic stiffness — forces vary (interrupted cutting, imbalance, gear vibration, chatter) → prevents resonance/amplification.
  • High damping — absorbs vibration energy → less chatter/amplitude/noise/roughness (why beds are cast iron).
  • High wear resistance (sliding parts) and good manufacturability (easy to cast/machine/assemble).
Design procedure & principles

Mark all external forces (location + direction) → identify critical sections (max bending/torsion/shear) → analyse mainly as bending in two perpendicular planes + torsion → design for bending stiffness, then torsional stiffness, verify strength, pick material + section form.

Stiffness rules: increase moment of inertia · reduce unsupported length · use closed sections · avoid thin plates · maintain symmetry. Forms: closed-frame (heavy milling/CNC — very rigid) and box structures (most modern — large moment of inertia, high stiffness, good load distribution). Loads: cutting force, dynamic amplification (harmonic), inertial/peak-torque at start/stop. Thermal: symmetry, thermal barriers, cooling.

Specific members (bed, column, base/table, cross-rail, arm, ram)
  • Bed — analysed for bending + torsion; reduced bending rigidity with perpendicular & diagonal stiffeners; lathe bed checked for normal bending stresses (vertical + horizontal) and section properties.
  • Columns — checked for shearing deformation and torsion (twist angle → guideway displacement).
  • Bases & tables — the table supports the work; the base = a stationary table; tables are rectangular or circular.
  • Cross rails — vertical ribs + horizontal stiffeners → higher bending/torsional/dynamic stiffness.
  • Arms/traverses (single-column) — near uniform-strength beams, analysed as cantilevers under combined bending + torsion (one plane for a radial drill, two for a single-column planer).
  • Ram — carries the tool/spindle head, gives linear motion (shapers/planers/slotters); cutting force far from support → cantilever; flexible ram → tool-point deflection (error) + low dynamic stiffness (chatter) → designed for bending + torsional stiffness.
Guideways — functions

Guideways guide moving parts (table, carriage, saddle, cross-slide, spindle head) along a predetermined path. Functions: provide accurate motion (deviation → taper/dimensional errors) · carry cutting + weight + dynamic loads · absorb vibration · maintain alignment · reduce friction.

Guideway classification & stick-slip
  • Sliding — direct contact + lubrication: simple, high load capacity, good damping, cheap; but high friction, stick-slip, wear.
  • Rolling — balls/rollers: very low friction, smooth, high positioning accuracy; but low damping, lower load capacity, expensive (CNC/precision).
  • Hydrostatic — oil pumped under pressure → film, zero metal contact, very low friction, high accuracy, excellent damping; but expensive, needs a pump (ultra-precision/grinding).
  • Aerostatic — pressurised air film: extremely low friction, no wear, very high precision; but very low load capacity, expensive (semiconductor/ultra-precision).

Stick-slip — static friction > kinetic friction → motion alternates sticking/sudden sliding → vibration, irregular motion, poor finish. Reduce with low-friction materials, lubrication, Turcite coatings, or rolling guideways.

Shapes: flat (easy, simple; poor alignment) · V (good alignment, prevents lateral motion) · dovetail (prevents lifting — milling slides, tool posts) · combination (lathe = flat + V → alignment + load support).

Slideway design (force analysis, pressure, stiffness)

Design requirements: high accuracy, long life, low friction, adequate stiffness, wear resistance, damping (cast iron / polymer concrete), proper lubrication (oil bath / forced / grease).

Slideways are designed from the forces on the mating surfaces for two cases — a combination of V + flat slideways (Figure A) and a combination of two flat slideways (Figure B) — which give the reactions, then the pressure distribution, checked against permissible maximum and average pressures. Also design of slideways for stiffness (two-flat and flat+V) using contact compliance and approximate design formulae.

MODULE 4 & 5

Spindles & Spindle Supports

Spindle functions, assemblies, ends & materials

A spindle is the rotating shaft holding/driving the work or tool. Functions: support the rotating element, transmit power/motion, maintain alignment, withstand cutting forces, achieve accuracy & finish. Weak spindle → vibration; excess deflection → reduced accuracy; poor design → reduced tool/bearing life.

  • Lathe spindle — holds/rotates work; carries chuck/faceplate/gears/pulley; usually hollow (bars pass through); radial + axial loads; low runout.
  • Drilling spindle — rotates drill + axial feed; mainly torsion + thrust; stiffness to avoid drill wandering.
  • Comparison: lathe → high rotational accuracy; milling → resist fluctuating loads (high stiffness); drilling → axial thrust + guidance.
  • Spindle ends — front end locates/centres tool/chuck/faceplate/arbor; taper standardized per machine for mounting, alignment, motion transmission.
  • Materials — rolled stock <150 mm, castings (centrifugal) >150 mm. Stiffness, not strength is the criterion (steels share similar E). Normal accuracy → plain carbon (C45/C59) hardened+tempered; above-normal → low-alloy induction-hardened; precision (sliding bearings) → low-alloy case-hardened/nitrided; hollow heavy-duty → grey/SG cast iron.
Compliance, accuracy & spindle deflection

Effect of machine-tool compliance on accuracy: deflection of the centres and workpiece, the maximum/minimum displacement along the work, and the effect of saddle compliance all shift the tool–work position → machining error.

Spindle deflection is built from: bending of the spindle axis; compliance of the supports (front + rear deflect → final support-compliance deflection); optimum spacing between supports (an optimum span for minimum nose deflection, depending on stated factors); and compliance of the tapered joint (with taper-angle errors reducing stiffness → measures to improve it). Plus permissible deflection + strength check, axial & torsional stiffness, and spindles under combined bending and torsion.

Anti-friction bearing supports

Overall spindle stiffness depends strongly on the front and rear support compliance. Supports need high guiding accuracy, good operation under varying conditions, high stiffness, minimum heating, good vibration stability (damping).

  • Anti-friction (rolling) bearings — low frictional moment/heat, low starting resistance, high load capacity per width, easy maintenance, low lubricant use.
  • Ball vs roller: balls — less heating, higher speed, cheaper, less alignment-sensitive; rollers — higher load capacity, when stiffness/load matter more than speed.
  • Combinations judged on performance (radial/axial stiffness, radial/axial runout, heat, max speed, thermal deformation, manufacturability). Taper-roller → more runout; only-cylindrical-roller front → more axial thermal deformation at the nose; angular-contact ball front → low heat + fair axial stiffness → high speed. Selection is always a compromise.
Preloading

Clearance fit → spindle shifts before rolling elements take load; on load reversal the deformation jumps → unstable position, accuracy loss. Interference/preload → removes the jump, smooth deformation–load relation, stiffer, predictable, better accuracy.

Why stiffer: preload restrains the support, shifting behaviour from simply-supported toward clamped (a clamped beam is stiffer) → nose deflection drops. Excessive preload → more heat, larger contact deformation, reduced bearing life → preload must be optimum. Preloading = an initial internal load (small relative axial displacement between inner/outer races) before the working load, applied via paired ball/angular-contact bearings; constant preload set during assembly by grinding race faces or controlled-length spacers.

Sliding bearing supports

Used when very high accuracy or very high speed is required, or under shocks/vibration — key advantage is inherent damping. Classified by lubrication:

  • Sleeve — very thin film, metal-to-metal contact, semi-liquid friction, designed for wear resistance (limit bearing pressure and pressure×speed).
  • Hydrodynamic journal — a complete film separates journal and bearing by hydrodynamic action from rotation.
  • Hydrostatic journal — lubricant supplied under external pressure.

Covered with the Hersey–Stribeck relationship and the working principle of a sliding journal bearing; hydrodynamic design uses the transition condition to full-film lubrication, the critical Sommerfeld number, recommended clearances, load capacity and eccentricity ratio.

MODULE 6

Vibration in Machine Tools

What vibration is & its sources

Vibration = unwanted oscillatory motion of components/tool/work/structure during machining. Even a tiny tool-tip vibration leaves visible marks; it affects accuracy, finish, tool life, machine life, productivity, cutting stability.

Sources: misalignment of shafts/couplings · gear errors (profile/pitch error, eccentricity, backlash, bad meshing, worn teeth) · bearing defects (wear, bad lubrication, excess clearance, wrong preload, poor mounting, contamination) · cutting-force variation (chip-thickness change, tool wear, interrupted cut, non-uniform material, built-up edge) · tool wear/geometry (blunt tool, bad rake/clearance, large overhang, poor clamping) · workpiece flexibility (long/slender/thin parts) · structural flexibility · external disturbances (nearby machines, floor/foundation, impacts, poor installation).

Types of vibration & chatter
  • Free — transient, after a disturbance, decays with damping.
  • Forced — from a periodic external force; worst at resonance (forcing ≈ natural frequency).
  • Self-excited — generated and sustained by the cutting process itself (no external periodic force); energy from cutting.
  • Chatter — self-excited, from tool/work/chip/structure interaction → wavy surface + loud noise.
  • Primary chatter — instability in the cutting process (friction, chip-formation instability, force variation, built-up edge).
  • Regenerative chatter (most common/important) — waviness left in one revolution/tooth pass changes the chip thickness on the next cut → fluctuating force → growing amplitude.
Effects of vibration
  • Accuracy — taper, ovality, out-of-roundness, poor flatness/cylindricity/straightness.
  • Surface finish — wavy/rough, chatter marks. Roughness vs waviness: roughness = small closely-spaced irregularities (feed marks, geometry); waviness = larger widely-spaced undulations from vibration/chatter/deflection (matters more in vibration problems).
  • Tool life — fluctuating force → faster flank/crater wear, chipping, micro-cracks, breakage, thermal fatigue; edge chipping (carbide/ceramic).
  • Machine — damages bearings, spindle, guideways, lead screws, gears, fixtures; loosens fasteners, lost alignment; bearing/spindle wear → runout → accuracy loss.
  • More heat (rubbing/friction → thermal distortion), noise (first warning sign), reduced productivity (conservative cutting → time, rework, rejects), higher power consumption, safety risks.
  • Examples: turning slender shaft → diameter varies; milling → waviness + tooth chipping; drilling → oversize/deviated holes.
Vibration control
  • Reduce the source first (most effective) — balance rotating parts, correct alignment, replace damaged bearings, improve gear quality, reduce backlash, improve lubrication, tighten loose parts, improve foundation. (Otherwise you only treat the symptom.)
  • Increase stiffness — box/ribbed structures, thicker sections, less overhang, rigid joints, shorter tool holders, support long work, wider guideways, better spindle support. (Reducing tool overhang greatly cuts chatter.)
  • Increase damping — cast-iron beds, polymer concrete, damping layers, viscous/friction damping, tuned absorbers, damping pads.
  • Avoid resonance — change the natural frequency away from the forcing frequency.
  • Cutting conditions — adjust speed/feed/depth/geometry/fluid; excessive depth → chatter; very low speed → built-up-edge instability.
  • Tool geometry — sharp tools, proper rake/clearance, optimum nose radius (large → better finish but more radial force/chatter), avoid overhang, rigid holders.
  • Workpiece support — tailstock, steady rest, follower rest, less unsupported length, good chucking/fixtures, support thin plates.
  • Clamping — clamp tool + work firmly; loose clamping → micro-movement → chatter.
  • Foundation & isolation — heavy blocks, anti-vibration mounts, levelling, isolation from heavy machines; isolators (rubber/spring/cork/elastomeric/pneumatic) cut transmission — but too-soft supports reduce stability.
  • Dampers — viscous, friction, constrained-layer, tuned-mass dampers, dynamic vibration absorbers (when stiffness alone can't fix it).
  • Chatter control — change spindle speed, reduce depth/feed, raise stiffness/damping, sharp tools, less overhang, better support, change geometry, variable-pitch cutters. For regenerative chatter, changing spindle speed is effective because it changes the phase between the previous waviness and the current cut.
  • Process-specific — turning/milling/drilling each have their own control checklists (overhang, holders, supports, depth, speed, geometry, alignment).
  • Monitoring — accelerometers, displacement sensors, acoustic-emission, force sensors, spindle-current sensors → detect chatter/bearing defects/tool wear/imbalance/misalignment → preventive maintenance.
  • Active vs passive — active (sensor+controller+actuator+feedback applies a counter-force; precise but costly) vs passive (stiffness/damping/isolation; cheaper, no power).
