Section A — Short answers

Answer briefly (2–4 marks each). Mixed CLOs.

Q1.CLO1[2]

Define the directrix and name the motion that produces it when shaping a flat surface.

Model answer
Directrix = the path along which the generatrix is guided. In shaping, the directrix (the flat's width) is produced by the intermittent feed motion of the table (the generatrix being the line cut by the ram stroke).
Q2.CLO2[4]

A gearbox must give 6 speeds from 45 to 1000 rpm. Find the step ratio φ (nearest standard) and write the speed series.

Model answer
φ = (nmax/nmin)1/(z−1) = (1000/45)1/5 = (22.2)0.2 ≈ 1.86 → nearest standard φ = 1.78 (or 2.0). With φ = 1.78: 45, 80, 142, 253, 450, 800 rpm (≈). State that exact end-to-end fit may need the next standard φ; the method is the marks.
Q3.CLO3[4]

Slideways are designed for wear and for stiffness. State both governing conditions and explain why the average pressure is often used instead of the maximum.

Model answer
Wear: pmax ≤ [pmax]; stiffness: deflection δ ≤ [δ] in the accuracy-critical directions. Because finding the true pmax from the pressure distribution is complicated, the design is simplified by limiting the average pressure pav ≤ [pav] over the bearing face (for cast iron, [pav] ≈ ½ of [pmax]) — a quicker, safe check.
Q4.CLO5[3]

Distinguish surface roughness from waviness. Which one is the signature of vibration?

Model answer
Roughness = small, closely-spaced irregularities from feed marks/tool geometry/edge condition. Waviness = larger, widely-spaced undulations from vibration, chatter, deflection or machine instability. Waviness (chatter marks) is the signature of vibration.
Q5.CLO2[4]

Why must every gear step in a speed box satisfy ¼ ≤ i ≤ 2?

Model answer
The lower limit i ≥ ¼ caps the speed reduction per stage so the driven gear (and its centre distance/size) does not become excessive; the upper limit i ≤ 2 caps the speed increase so pitch-line velocity, gear forces and noise stay acceptable and the small pinion is not over-stressed. Keeping ratios within this band gives compact, reliable, quiet gearing; a wider range is obtained by adding stages instead.

Section B — Long answers

Answer in detail (5–6 marks each). The synthesis questions.

Q6.CLO4[6]

The total deflection of a spindle nose has three components. Identify them, state what each depends on, and say which usually dominates.

Model answer
  • y₁ — bending of the spindle under the gear force P₂ and nose force P₁; depends on the spindle EI, span l and overhang c (Macaulay's analysis).
  • y₂ — compliance of the supports (front + rear bearings); depends on the bearing stiffnesses and is projected to the nose by the overhang: y₂ = δB(1+c/l) + δA(c/l).
  • y₃ — compliance of the tapered joint seating the tool/centre.
Total y = y₁ + y₂ + y₃. The support compliance y₂ usually dominates the nose deflection (the front bearing, amplified by the 1+c/l factor), which is why bearing stiffness, preload and span optimisation matter most.
Q7.CLO4[5]

Explain the idea of an optimum bearing span-to-overhang ratio λ = l/c for minimum spindle-nose deflection. What trades off, and why must λ ≥ 2.5 for anti-friction bearings?

Model answer
As the span l grows, the bending deflection y₁ rises but the support-compliance deflection y₂ falls (reactions on the bearings drop); the sum y₁+y₂ has a minimum at an optimum λ = l/c, typically 3–5, read from the design chart using α = KB/KA. A lower bound λ ≥ 2.5 is imposed for anti-friction bearings because below it the bearing radial play produces excessive run-out of the nose; an upper bound comes from keeping the span stiffness adequate for the drive gear.
Q8.CLO5[6]

Explain mode-coupling chatter and how it differs from regenerative chatter.

Model answer
Mode coupling: when the structure has two coupled vibration directions of similar frequency, the tool tip traces an elliptical path. Over one loop the chip is thicker while moving in the cutting-force direction and thinner on return, so the cutting force does net positive work on the structure each cycle → energy is pumped in and vibration self-sustains, even on a virgin surface (no previous wave needed). Regenerative chatter instead needs the wavy surface left by the previous pass; the phase between successive waves drives instability and it is speed-dependent (stability lobes). Mode coupling depends on the directional stiffness (rigidity ellipse) and coupling of modes, not on the surface-regeneration delay.
Q9.CLO5[4]

Explain why the limiting depth of cut for regenerative chatter is roughly half that for primary (mode-coupling) chatter.

Model answer
Stability is judged by where the open-loop response crosses the negative-real axis. For primary chatter the threshold is the (−1, 0) point, but the regenerative term y(t) − m·y(t−τ) doubles the effective feedback in the worst phase, moving the critical crossing to the (−½, 0) line. Since blim is inversely proportional to that critical real part, the regenerative limiting width is about half of the primary one — regenerative chatter is therefore more restrictive and harder to avoid.
Q10.CLO4[5]

Define machine-tool compliance and explain how the machine and the workpiece combine to set the total ("system") compliance and the resulting accuracy.

Model answer
Compliance = deflection per unit force (1/stiffness). The headstock centre, tailstock centre and saddle/tool each contribute; at the cutting point their effect combines (position-weighted) into the machine-tool compliance. The workpiece itself bends as a beam between the centres, adding its own compliance in series: Csystem = Cmachine + Cworkpiece. The tool–work relative deflection = Csystem × cutting force, which varies along the length → diameter/taper error. Accuracy is improved by stiffening the softest link (often a bearing/centre) and by balancing the centre stiffnesses.
Q11.CLO3[4]

Stick-slip ruins fine positioning on a precision slide. Explain the mechanism and recommend a guideway solution with justification.

Model answer
Mechanism: μstatic > μkinetic, so at low feed the slide sticks, the drive elastically winds up, then breaks free and jumps — jerky motion and position error. Solution: replace plain sliding ways with rolling (LM) or hydrostatic guideways, or face the ways with PTFE/Turcite, so kinetic friction is ≥ static (no negative friction–velocity slope) and the film/rolling contact removes stiction. This restores smooth, repeatable micro-feed; raising drive stiffness helps further.
Q12.CLO1[4]

Distinguish working motions from auxiliary motions and illustrate both across turning, drilling and milling.

Model answer
Working motions directly accomplish cutting (primary cutting + feed); auxiliary motions set up or support the cut without removing material (rapid approach/retract, indexing, tool change, clamping, depth setting). Turning: working = work rotation + tool feed; auxiliary = cross-slide setting, tailstock clamping, rapid return. Drilling: working = drill rotation + axial feed; auxiliary = table positioning, drill retract. Milling: working = cutter rotation + table feed; auxiliary = table indexing, rapid traverse, work clamping.
