CLO1

Drives, Process Kinematics & Working Motions

Q1.[2]

Define generatrix and directrix.

Model answer
Generatrix — the line/profile that, when produced, forms the surface. Directrix — the path along which the generatrix is guided. The machined surface is the generatrix swept along the directrix (e.g. turning: generatrix = a line along the axis, directrix = the circle traced by rotation).
Q2.[4]

List the four methods of generating a surface, with one machine-tool example of each.

Model answer
  • Tracing (forming by feed) — turning a cylinder (tool traces the profile as it feeds).
  • Forming — a form tool reproduces its own profile (form turning, gear by form cutter).
  • Copying — a stylus follows a template (copy turning, profile milling).
  • Generation — a rolling/enveloping motion produces the surface (gear hobbing, gear shaping).
Q3.[3]

State the basic functional elements common to any machine tool.

Model answer
Drive system (motor, belts, gears); structure (bed, frame, column); tool-holding mechanism; workpiece-holding mechanism; control/feed system. Each contributes to holding tool and work in a controlled relative motion with accuracy and rigidity.
Q4.[3]

Distinguish primary (cutting), feed and auxiliary motions; state the working motions in turning, drilling and milling.

Model answer
Primary = high-speed chip-removal motion; feed = slow motion sustaining the cut; auxiliary = non-cutting (setting, indexing, rapid traverse, clamping). Turning: primary = work rotation, feed = tool travel. Drilling: primary = drill rotation, feed = drill axial. Milling: primary = cutter rotation, feed = table travel.
CLO2

Regulation of Speed & Feed

Q5.[3]

State why spindle speeds are arranged in geometric progression and list the standard values of the step ratio φ.

Model answer
GP keeps the percentage speed loss between adjacent steps constant over the whole range → economical cutting speed at every diameter. Standard φ (from the R-series preferred numbers): 1.06, 1.12, 1.26, 1.41, 1.58, 1.78, 2.0.
Q6.[3]

Define step ratio φ, number of speeds z and range ratio Rn. Write Rn in terms of φ and z.

Model answer
φ = nk+1/nk (constant ratio between successive speeds); z = number of spindle speeds; Rn = nmax/nmin (range ratio). Relation: Rn = φ(z−1), hence φ = (nmax/nmin)1/(z−1).
Q7.[3]

State the permissible transmission-ratio limits for a speed gearbox, and name the two diagrams used in its design.

Model answer
Limits: ¼ ≤ i ≤ 2 (a gear step may reduce speed by at most 4:1 and raise it by at most 2:1) to keep gear sizes/forces reasonable. Diagrams: the structural (ray) diagram (lays out speed steps and ratios per shaft) and the speed diagram (actual rpm on a log scale).
CLO3

Guideways — Functions & Requirements

Q8.[3]

List the principal functions of a machine-tool guideway.

Model answer
Provide accurate guided motion along a defined path; carry loads (cutting force, weight of moving member, dynamic loads); maintain the geometric alignment between tool and workpiece.
Q9.[4]

List the requirements of a good guideway.

Model answer
Geometric accuracy of motion; high stiffness/rigidity; wear resistance with constant accuracy over life; good damping; low friction (no stick-slip); ease of manufacture, adjustment and protection (gibs, wipers, covers).
Q10.[4]

Classify guideways by contact type and give one advantage of each.

Model answer
  • Sliding — simple, cheap, high damping.
  • Rolling — very low friction, no stick-slip, high positioning accuracy.
  • Hydrostatic — oil film, near-zero wear/friction, excellent damping.
  • Aerostatic — air film, frictionless, for ultra-precision/light loads.
Q11.[3]

State the two design criteria for slideways and the governing condition for each.

Model answer
Wear resistance → bearing pressure limited: pmax ≤ [pmax] (or pav ≤ [pav]). Stiffness → deflection of the cutting edge limited: δ ≤ [δ] in the directions that affect accuracy.
CLO4

Strength & Stiffness of Structures

Q12.[3]

State why stiffness — not strength — is the governing criterion in machine-tool structure and spindle design.

Model answer
Machining accuracy is lost through elastic deflection long before any stress limit is reached, so deflection (rigidity) governs. For spindles all steels share nearly the same modulus E, so a stronger alloy adds no stiffness — material/section is chosen for rigidity, wear and accuracy, designing to a permissible deflection [y].
Q13.[3]

List the three components that make up the deflection of a spindle nose.

Model answer
y₁ — deflection due to bending of the spindle under the forces P₁, P₂; y₂ — deflection due to compliance of the front and rear supports (bearings); y₃ — deflection due to compliance of the tapered joint (tool/centre seating). Total y = y₁ + y₂ + y₃.
Q14.[4]

State the functions and key requirements of a spindle, and list the recommended materials.

Model answer
Functions: support and rotate the work/tool, transmit power and motion, maintain alignment and rotational accuracy, withstand cutting forces. Requirements: rotational accuracy (low run-out), high stiffness, wear resistance at the nose/bearings. Materials: plain-carbon C45/C50 (normal accuracy); low-alloy case-hardened/nitrided steels (precision); grey/SG cast iron for hollow heavy-duty spindles.
Q15.[2]

Define machine-tool compliance.

Model answer
Compliance = elastic deflection produced per unit applied force = 1/stiffness (e.g. µm/kgf). The compliances of saddle, headstock and tailstock combine at the cutting point to give the tool-point error.
CLO5

Vibration — Sources & Elimination

Q16.[3]

Classify the vibrations occurring in machine tools.

Model answer
Free/transient (after a shock, decays with damping); forced (sustained by a periodic external force); self-excited / chatter (energy drawn from the cutting process itself) — sub-divided into primary and regenerative chatter.
Q17.[4]

List the main sources of forced vibration in a machine tool.

Model answer
Unbalance of rotating parts; shaft/coupling misalignment; gear errors (profile, pitch, backlash, wear); bearing defects; cutting-force variation / interrupted cutting; hydraulic & pump pulsations; external disturbances transmitted through the foundation.
Q18.[4]

List the methods used to suppress or eliminate machine-tool vibration.

Model answer
Reduce the source (balance, align, replace bearings, improve gears); increase static & dynamic stiffness (box/ribbed sections, shorter overhang, preload); increase damping (cast-iron/polymer-concrete beds, dampers); fit a dynamic vibration absorber / tuned-mass damper; isolate the foundation (anti-vibration mounts); choose stable cutting parameters (spindle speed on a stable lobe, lower depth, sharp tool, variable-pitch cutters).
