CLO1 — Drives, Working Motions & Kinematics

Module 1 · Mehta ch 1 · ≈ 10 marks. Identify the drives, process kinematics and working motions of machine tools.

1. Working and Auxiliary Motions

Definition. A machine tool imparts the required shape to a workpiece, with the desired accuracy, by removing metal as chips. The relative motions between cutting edge and workpiece that are essential to impart the shape are called working motions.

Working motions are geometrically rotary or translatory, and functionally:

  1. Drive / primary cutting motion — carries the cutting edge through the material and removes the chip. Its velocity is the cutting speed v (m/min); it absorbs most of the cutting power.
  2. Feed motion — progressively brings fresh material into the cutting edge's path so the cut spreads over the whole surface. Its velocity is the feed s (small).

Millingrotation of the cuttertranslatory motion of the workpiece
Shaping / planingreciprocation of tool (shaper) / work (planer)intermittent feed (once per stroke)
Grindingrotation of the wheelrotary and translatory feed of work
Exam point. In shaping/planing the feed is intermittent (cutting only on the forward stroke). In grinding the feed has two components (longitudinal traverse + radial in-feed). Auxiliary motions (clamping, idle rapid travel, gear shifting, engaging/disengaging) do not form the surface — manual on general machines, automated on automatics.

3. Machine Tool Drives

Definition. A machine tool drive is the aggregate of mechanisms that transmits motion from the energy source (usually a 3-φ induction motor) to the operative elements, providing the working/auxiliary motion and a means of speed regulation.

Classification — by speed regulation

TypeSpeedCharacter
SteppedFinite discrete stepsMechanical gearboxes (sliding-gear, clutch)
Stepless (infinitely variable)ContinuousHydraulic & electrical (always); mechanical variators

Classification — by means of transmission

  • Mechanical — gears/belts/chains/screws; stepped or stepless.
  • Hydraulic — pump/valve/cylinder circuit; always stepless; smooth, easy reversal, overload protection; but oil leakage & viscosity/temperature dependence.
  • Electrical — variable-speed electric circuits; always stepless; ideal for NC/CNC; costlier control gear.
Exam point — why stepless is desirable. The optimum cutting speed from v = πdn/1000 usually falls between two stepped speeds, forcing the operator to the nearest lower rpm and lengthening machining time. A stepless drive sets the exact optimum → higher productivity.

5. Kinematic Structure — Chains & Diagram

Definition. A kinematic chain is the full sequence of transmission elements (gears, belts, screws, clutches) connecting a motion source to an operative element, fixing the velocity relationship. The set of all chains = the kinematic structure, shown by a kinematic diagram.
  • Speed (main) chain: motor → speed gearbox → spindle → work; sets cutting speed.
  • Feed chain: spindle (or feed motor) → feed gearbox → lead screw / feed rod → carriage; sets feed (tied to spindle for threading).

Motion passes through elementary transmissions in series; overall ratio = product of individual ratios. Gear pair: n₂ = n₁·(Z₁/Z₂). Ratio >1 increases speed, <1 reduces (worm pair always reduces, self-locking).

Exam point. Kinematic accuracy (e.g. spindle-rotation vs lead-screw travel in threading) improves with shorter chains and accurate gears — fewer pairs = fewer accumulated errors.

6. Mechanisms & Lathe Layout

  • Sliding-gear (cluster) — a gear block shifted on a splined shaft to change ratio (stepped speeds).
  • Clutch type — gears permanently mesh; ratio selected by a clutch (jaw = no slip but engage at rest; friction/electromagnetic = engage at speed, used in NC).
  • Quick-return (shaper/slotter): cutting stroke slow, return fast. Slider-crank (small strokes, return not much faster) vs crank-and-rocker / Whitworth (cutting over larger angle α, return over smaller β → true quick return, large strokes).
  • Lathe (formula AOXYcwd): A = work rotation (primary motion); O = bed; X = carriage feed; Y = cross-slide feed; c,w,d = tool-post setting motions. Elements: bed, headstock (spindle + speed box), tailstock, carriage/saddle+apron, cross/compound slide, tool post, feed box + lead screw.

TQ1 — Working vs auxiliary motion CLO1

Q: Define working motion and auxiliary motion in a machine tool. (2 marks)

Show answer ▸

Working motions are the relative motions between cutting edge and workpiece essential to impart the shape (they form the surface) — the drive/primary cutting motion + the feed motion. Auxiliary motions do not form the surface but are needed for the working motions to function — clamping/unclamping, idle rapid travel, gear (speed) changing, engaging/disengaging.

TQ2 — Primary vs feed motion CLO1

Q: Distinguish drive (primary cutting) motion from feed motion and identify each for a lathe, drill and mill. (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

Drive/primary cutting motion carries the cutting edge through the material and removes the chip; velocity = cutting speed v (m/min); absorbs most cutting power. Feed motion brings fresh material into the path so the cut spreads; velocity = feed s, comparatively small. Lathe: primary = work rotation; feed = tool translation. Drill: primary = drill rotation; feed = axial drill travel. Mill: primary = cutter rotation; feed = workpiece translation.

TQ3 — Methods of generating surfaces CLO1

Q: Explain the methods of producing surfaces on machine tools, one example each. (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

Forming — edge carries the profile, simple feed reproduces it (form-tool turning). Generating — surface = envelope of cutting-edge positions via coordinated motion (gear hobbing). Tracing — single-point tool lays surface line-by-line (plain turning). Copying — tool follows a template/master/NC (copy turning, CNC contour). Contrast: forming copies tool profile; generating builds an envelope; tracing lays line-by-line; copying follows a master.

TQ4 — Stepped vs stepless drives CLO1

Q: Classify drives by speed regulation and by transmission. Why is a stepless drive advantageous? (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

By regulation: stepped (discrete steps, mechanical gearboxes) vs stepless (continuous; hydraulic, electrical, mechanical variators). By transmission: mechanical (stepped or stepless), hydraulic (always stepless), electrical (always stepless). Advantage of stepless: the optimum speed v = πdn/1000 usually lies between two steps; a stepped drive forces the nearest lower rpm, lengthening machining time. Stepless sets the exact optimum → maximum productivity and finish.

TQ5 — Hydraulic vs electrical vs mechanical drives CLO1

Q: Compare hydraulic, electrical and mechanical stepless drives (advantages/disadvantages). (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

Hydraulic: + smooth stepless speed/feed, easy reversal, rotary & translatory motion, overload protection; − oil leakage, speed varies with oil temp/viscosity, costlier, lower efficiency. Electrical: + stepless, easily automated, ideal for NC/CNC, clean; − costly control gear, plain induction motor needs extra equipment for variable speed. Mechanical: + simple, robust, no power conversion; − stepped versions can't hit exact optimum, variators slip/wear and have limited range.

TQ6 — Types of drive CLO1

Q: Differentiate individual, group (line-shaft) and multi-motor drives. (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

Group/line-shaft: one motor drives many machines via an overhead shaft & belts — obsolete (high losses, all stop together, unsafe). Individual: each machine has its own motor & gearbox — independent control, efficient, safe, standard practice. Multi-motor: one machine uses several motors (spindle, feed, coolant, rapid traverse) — shortens kinematic chains, independent optimised control; common on large/automatic/CNC machines.

TQ7 — Quick-return mechanisms CLO1

Q: Why is a quick-return mechanism needed in shaping/slotting? Compare slider-crank with crank-and-rocker. (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

Metal is cut only on the forward stroke; the return is idle, so making the return faster raises productivity. Slider-crank: forward and reverse each take half a crank revolution → return not appreciably faster; small strokes (<300 mm). Crank-and-rocker (Whitworth): crank turns a rocker; cutting over the larger angle α, return over the smaller β (α>β) → genuinely faster return; large strokes (up to ~1000 mm), used in shapers/slotters.

TQ8 — Kinematic structure & lathe layout (long) CLO1

Q: Explain the kinematic structure of a machine tool — speed & feed chains, how motion is transmitted/regulated, and the layout/elements of a lathe. (10 marks)

Show answer ▸

Structure: a drive is an aggregate of mechanisms transmitting motion from a 3-φ motor to the operative elements; the sequence connecting a source to an operative element is a kinematic chain, the set of all chains is the structure (shown by a kinematic diagram). Speed chain: motor → speed gearbox → spindle (sets cutting speed). Feed chain: spindle/feed-motor → feed box → lead screw/feed rod → carriage (sets feed; tied to spindle for threading). Transmission/regulation: elementary transmissions in series, overall ratio = product; gear pair n₂=n₁(Z₁/Z₂); regulated in steps (sliding-gear/clutch) or steplessly (hydraulic/electrical); shorter chains → better kinematic accuracy. Lathe (AOXYcwd): A = work rotation (primary), O = bed, X = carriage feed, Y = cross feed, c,w,d = tool-post setting. Elements: bed, headstock (spindle+speed box), tailstock, carriage/saddle+apron, cross/compound slide, tool post, feed box + lead screw. A lathe shows all three motion classes at once.

CLO2 — Regulation of Speed & Feed Rates

Module 2 · Mehta ch 2 · ≈ 10 marks. Deduce the means to regulate speed and feed in machine tools.

1. Aim of Regulation

Every operation must run at the cutting parameters (v, s, t) that give the minimum cost of the part. To machine any diameter at the optimum cutting speed the spindle rpm must be settable to n = 1000v/(πD).

Definition. Stepped regulation provides only discrete rpm/feed values (geared drive). Stepless regulation allows any value in the range, so the tool always runs at the exact optimum. Most machines use stepped regulation (stepless is not yet economical for all).

2. Why Geometric Progression?

The worst loss of economic cutting speed is when vopt sits midway between two speeds nj, nj+1:

(Δv)_max = [(φ − 1)/(φ + 1)]·v_opt , φ = n_(j+1)/n_j
Exam point. This loss depends only on the step ratio φ. For it to be constant over the whole range, φ must be constant → speeds must be a geometric progression. GP also gives constant productivity loss (Q = 1000·s·v) and lets one gearbox make the whole series by changing gear pairs.

Compared: arithmetic — step ratio falls ~2 → 1.09, high range redundant; harmonic — constant diameter range but high-range speeds too far apart; logarithmic — best operationally but impractical (trial-built). GP is the practical compromise.

5. Number of Teeth

All gears of a group share the same module → sum of teeth on each pair is constant, Z₁+Z₂ = C. Start from the minimum-ratio pair (set Zmin ≥ 20 in speed boxes), then Z₁ = C·i/(1+i), Z₂ = C/(1+i), rounded. Actual rpm must deviate ≤ ±10(φ−1)%.

6. Feed Regulation & Special Mechanisms

Feed boxes generally use GP feeds (constant productivity loss). Exceptions: thread-cutting pitches form an arithmetic progression; ratchet feeds (shaping/grinding) are intermittent. Feed limits are wider: i ≤ 2.8, i ≥ 1/5.

Other feed methods: change gears (precise, slow to set), sliding gears (large torque), gear cone + sliding key, jaw/friction clutches.

7. Stepless Drives

Allow exact-optimum speed and change-without-stopping. Hydraulic (variable-delivery pump / throttle, Rv 50–60). Electrical (vary frequency/poles/slip; DC shunt, Ward-Leonard Rv 8–16; modern VVVF). Mechanical variators — friction faceplate/cone, and the PIV (Positive Infinitely Variable) cone-pulley + laminated chain: positive no-slip drive, Rv 10, η 0.9.

8. Thread Cutting & Indexing Kinematics

Thread cutting (lathe): the lead-screw is kinematically tied to the spindle so that in one spindle revolution the tool advances exactly one thread pitch: pitch of work = (spindle rev) × (lead-screw lead) × i, i = change-gear ratio = (driver teeth)/(driven teeth) = pitchwork/pitchleadscrew. This is why thread pitches form an arithmetic progression (not GP). Indexing (milling, dividing head): for N divisions the crank turns 40/N revolutions per division (40:1 worm), using index-plate holes for fractions. Both are kinematic-accuracy chains — shorter chains and accurate change gears keep the ratio exact.

TQ5 — The three diagrams CLO2

Q: Differentiate the structural diagram, ray diagram and speed chart. (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

TQ6 — Number of teeth CLO2

Q: Explain how the number of teeth of gears in a group is determined. (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

All gears of a group share one module → equal centre distance → Z₁+Z₂ = C (constant). Start from the minimum-ratio pair, set the small gear to Zmin (≥20 speed box, ≥16 feed box, avoid undercutting). Then Z₁ = C·i/(1+i), Z₂ = C/(1+i), rounded to whole teeth; try several C, pick least adjustment; actual rpm deviation ≤ ±10(φ−1)%.

TQ7 — Norton vs Meander CLO2

Q: Describe the Norton (tumbler) and Meander feed mechanisms and compare them. (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

Norton: a cone of gears on the driving shaft; a tumbler gear on a swing arm is slid and meshed with any cone gear → direct precise ratios (exact thread pitches), compact, single-lever; poor rigidity, dirt entry. Meander: identical double-cluster gears; a tumbler meshes successively so ratios naturally form a GP; halves available ratios. Both compact, single-lever, used in thread-cutting lathes, same rigidity/accuracy flaws.

100 → 141 → 200 → 283 → 400 → 566 → 800 → 1131 → 1600 rpm
≈ standard: 100, 140, 200, 280, 400, 560, 800, 1120, 1600 rpm.

Step 3 — structural formula 9 = 3(1) × 3(3). Two gear stages in series: stage I has 3 ratios with characteristic x₁ = 1; stage II has 3 ratios with characteristic x₂ = 3. Total speeds = 3 × 3 = 9. The characteristic = number of speeds a stage "spans" before the next stage shifts.

1. Functions & Classification of Structures

Structures (beds, bases, columns, housings, tables, carriages, cross-rails, arms, rams) form the load-carrying skeleton: they support all sub-assemblies and keep the correct relative position of tool and workpiece under load, preserving accuracy over the machine's life.

GroupMembersFunction
1Beds, basesFoundation members carrying the whole machine, transmitting load to floor
2Box-type housings (speed/feed box, spindle head)Enclose & locate gear/bearing units
3Tables, carriages, saddles, knees, cross-rails, arms, ramsSupport/move workpiece or tool along guideways

2. Requirements of Structures

  1. High accuracy of mating/locating surfaces.
  2. Long-term dimensional stability (resist ageing, residual-stress relaxation).
  3. Strength — stresses & deformations (mechanical + thermal) within limits.
  4. High static & dynamic stiffness.
  5. Good damping.
  6. Low thermal deformation.
  7. High wear resistance of guideways (moving members).
  8. Manufacturability & economy of metal.
Exam point. These reduce to two fundamentals: (i) proper material selection, (ii) high static & dynamic stiffness. For moving members add (iii) wear resistance of guiding surfaces.

3. Strength vs Stiffness — Optimum l²/h

Strength volume V_σ = (3/2)(P/[σ])(l²/h) Stiffness volume V_δ = (P/4E[δ])(l²/h)² Optimum (V_σ=V_δ): (l²/h)_opt = 6E[δ]/[σ]
Exam point. Real machine tools have l²/h greater than the optimum → the Vδ curve dominates → stiffness, not strength, is the decisive sizing criterion. Design for stiffness first; check strength afterwards.

4. Materials

Selection index = unit rigidity E/γ. All steels & duralumin have nearly equal E/γ (~2.6×10⁸), so costly alloy steel gives no benefit; plain structural steel suffices. Grey CI E/γ ≈ 1.66×10⁸.

5. Static & Dynamic Stiffness

Definition. Static stiffness K = P/Y (force ÷ displacement in the force direction). Compliance C = 1/K. Dynamic stiffness Kdyn = K/A relates a time-varying force to displacement (A = magnification factor).
  • Series (force path): compliances add, C = C₁+C₂+C₃ → stiffen the weakest element; for shaft trains the last shaft dominates.
  • Parallel (equal deformation): stiffnesses add, K = K₁+K₂+K₃ → improve the strongest element.
  • Reduced by: apertures, joints (contact compliance), slender proportions. Dynamic stiffness also falls near resonance; raise damping & keep natural freq ≥ 2.5× highest excitation.

Exam point. I-section excels in bending (material far from neutral axis → large I) but is poor in torsion. Closed box excels in torsion because It ∝ A₀² (enclosed area squared): τ = Mt/(2A₀δ). Machine members carry combined bending + torsion → closed box preferred. Open: It = β·⅓Σbt³ (β: rect 1.0, channel 1.12, I 1.20).

Apertures cut stiffness (round hole disturbs ≈2d; torsional loss worst, recover bending with cover plates). Stiffeners: diagonal (Warren beam) best for both bending & torsion; perpendicular help bending only.

7. Design Procedure & Model Technique

Mark all forces → (1) design for bending stiffness, (2) torsional stiffness, (3) check strength (principal stress σp = σ/2 + √[(σ/2)²+τ²] ≤ [σ]); take the largest dimensions. Model technique: because exact analysis errs 30–50%, behaviour is predicted from small-scale Perspex models (E/G close to metals → ~3–5% error). Scaling: fs/fm = (1/λ)√(Esρm/Emρs). Perspex creeps above ~68 kgf/cm² → use a compensating ring.

TQ — Static stiffness & compliance CLO4

Q: Define static stiffness and compliance of a machine tool element. (2 marks)

Show answer ▸

Static stiffness K = P/Y = static force ÷ displacement in the force direction (slope dP/dY). Compliance C = 1/K = displacement per unit force. High K (low C) → small deflection under cutting force → better accuracy and dynamic stability.

TQ — Why cast iron for beds; CI vs welded steel CLO4

Q: Why is grey cast iron the traditional bed material? Compare with welded steel. (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

CI: high inherent damping (graphite), good sliding/wear, excellent castability of complex ribbed profiles (cored holes), cheap in large runs; but lower strength/E (bulkier) and min wall thickness limited by casting. Steel: higher strength & E/γ (thinner, lighter, deeper sections), easy repair/prototyping; but poor damping (welds only), inferior sliding, needs many ribs, machined holes. → Steel for simple heavily-loaded parts in small numbers; CI for complex parts in large numbers; combined cast-and-welded otherwise.

TQ — Closed box for torsion, open I for bending CLO4

Q: Justify why torsion-loaded beds are closed boxes while bending-loaded beds may be open I-sections. (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

Bending → I: stiffness ∝ I about the neutral axis; the I places material in flanges far from the axis → max I per unit area → min deflection. Torsion → closed box: for a thin closed section τ = Mt/(2A₀δ) and It ∝ A₀² (enclosed area squared); an open section has no enclosed area so It = β·⅓Σbt³ is tiny. Equal-area data: box twists 8.8–31.4× stiffer vs I-section 1.9×. Hence box for torsion, open I for bending.

TQ — Apertures & stiffeners CLO4

Q: How do apertures and ribs affect box-structure stiffness, and how are losses compensated? (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

Apertures cut the load path: a round hole disturbs ≈2d, an elongated one ≈l+d; width has the greatest effect. Bending loss (worst for holes in walls ⟂ to the bending plane) is recovered by bolted cover plates; torsional loss is more serious and needs a reduction factor (cover plates help only if bolts are tight enough). Stiffeners: simple longitudinal/transverse ribs add weight with little torsional gain; only diagonal stiffeners (Warren beam) improve both bending & torsion significantly.

TQ — Optimum l²/h; stiffness governs CLO4

Q: Show the optimum l²/h ratio and explain why stiffness (not strength) governs structure dimensions. (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

Vσ = (3/2)(P/[σ])(l²/h), Vδ = (P/4E[δ])(l²/h)²; setting Vσ=Vδ → (l²/h)opt = 6E[δ]/[σ]. Below it strength governs, above it stiffness governs. Real machine tools have l²/h > optimum, so the Vδ curve dominates → dimensions are fixed by deflection (stiffness), strength only checked. (So the ~13× steel-vs-CI metal saving from strength alone isn't realised — steel must be ribbed.)

TQ — Model technique & Perspex CLO4

Q: Describe the model technique in structure design. Why Perspex, and its limitations? (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

Exact analysis of complex profiles errs 30–50%, so a small-scale model (scale λ) is tested and the structure's behaviour predicted: fs/fm = (1/λ)√(Esρm/Emρs); validity needs similar geometry, loading and E/G (Poisson). Perspex: easily machined, transparent, E/G close to metals (~3–5% error). Limits: creeps above ~68 kgf/cm² (use a compensating ring); E varies with frequency; absorbs ~2% moisture lowering E ~10% (controlled humidity).

TQ — Functions, requirements, materials (long) CLO4

Q: Discuss the functions, requirements and materials of machine tool structures. (10 marks)

Show answer ▸

Functions: the load-carrying skeleton supporting sub-assemblies and maintaining tool–work relative position under load. Group 1 beds/bases (foundation), Group 2 housings (enclose gear/bearing units), Group 3 tables/saddles/cross-rails/rams (carry/move work or tool). Requirements: accurate mating surfaces; long-term stability; strength (mechanical+thermal); high static & dynamic stiffness; good damping; low thermal deformation; guideway wear resistance; manufacturability — reducing to material selection + stiffness (+ wear for moving members). Since l²/h > optimum, stiffness governs (design bending then torsion, check strength). Materials: index E/γ (all steels equal → plain steel fine). Grey CI: high damping, good sliding, castable, cheap, but bulky; welded steel: stronger/stiffer/lighter, easy repair, but poor damping, many ribs; combined cast-and-welded and polymer concrete also used. → steel for simple heavy parts in small numbers, CI for complex parts in large numbers.

Prob 3.1 CLO4

Given: Solid rectangular structure, height h, width b, length l, simply supported, loaded as Fig 3.32 (central load P + symmetric UDLs q₁, q₂). Permissible stress [σ], permissible deflection [δ], modulus E.

Find: Derive the optimum ratio l²/h.

Show solution ▸

Step 1 — strength volume. Mmax = Pl/4, zmax = h/2, I = bh³/12. σmax = (Pl/4)(h/2)/(bh³/12) = (3/2)·Pl/(bh²). Set σmax = [σ], metal volume Vσ = b·h·l: Vσ = (3/2)·(P/[σ])·(l²/h).

Step 2 — stiffness volume. δ = Pl³/(48EI). Set δ = [δ]: Vδ = (P/(4E[δ]))·(l²/h)².

Step 3 — optimum. Both criteria need the same metal: Vσ = Vδ → (3/2)(P/[σ])(l²/h) = (P/(4E[δ]))(l²/h)².

Step 4 — simplify. Cancel P and one (l²/h): (3/2)/[σ] = (1/(4E[δ]))·(l²/h).

Answer: (l²/h)opt = 6E[δ]/[σ]. If l²/h < opt → design for strength; if > opt (the usual machine-tool case) → design for stiffness.

Exam tip: derivation hinges on equating Vσ and Vδ; memorise (l²/h)opt = 6E[δ]/[σ].

Prob 3.3 CLO4

Given: Bed under torsion vs bed under bending. Compare closed box vs open (I/channel) section.

Find: prove why torsion beds are closed-box and bending beds can be open I-sections.

Show solution ▸

Torsion, closed: It = 4A₀²/(∮ds/δ), A₀ = enclosed area → huge It.

Torsion, open: It = β·⅓Σbt³ — no enclosed area, only thin strips cubed in small thickness → It,open ≪ It,closed (Table 3.3: box ~16× stiffer in torsion).

Bending: I = Σ(I + Ad²) — material far from neutral axis dominates; an open I places flanges at the extreme fibres just as well as a box (deflection nearly equal).

Answer: torsion → It ∝ A₀² so use closed box; bending → I ∝ Ad² (flange distance) independent of closure, so an open I-section does it with less metal.

Exam tip: one-line proof = torsion needs A₀² (closed), bending needs flange distance (open OK).

CLO3 (Module 3) — Design of Guideways & Power Screws

Mehta ch 4. Design machine tool guideways, identifying their functions and requirements.

1. Function & Requirements

Definition. A guideway guides a moving member (carriage, table, saddle, ram) along a predetermined path — linear (lathe, drilling, planing) or circular (rotary tables of VTLs/boring mills).

Requirements: (1) high accuracy & surface finish of the surfaces; (2) high accuracy of travel; (3) durability (retain initial accuracy); (4) low friction → less wear; (5) minimum variation of μ; (6) high rigidity/stiffness; (7) good damping. Plus: easy lubrication, wear compensation (gibs), wear-resistant material, protection from chips/dust.

Exam point. It is not the magnitude of μ but its variation (with speed and time-since-oiling) that ruins precision — it causes stick-slip. That's why liquid-friction / anti-friction ways replace plain slideways in precision machines. Cutting-force components: Pz tangential, Py radial, Px axial.

4. Design of Slideways

Designed for wear resistance (p ≤ [p]) and stiffness (δ ≤ [δ]). Resolve carriage equilibrium for reactions, e.g. two-flat ways:

B = P_y C = (P_z·y_p − P_y·h)/b + G/2 A = P_z + G/2 − (P_z·y_p − P_y·h)/b Pulling force Q = P_x + f(P_z + P_y + G) Face width on wear basis c' = A_reaction/([p_av]·L)
Exam point — no tipping. Every reaction must stay compressive (positive). If a reaction comes out negative, part of the carriage has lifted off — it tends to tip. An open way cannot resist this; a closed way carries the lift-off load. Permissible CI pressures: feed 25–30, planing/shaping 10–12, precision 1–2 kgf/cm² ([pav] ≈ ½[pmax]).

5. Friction, Stick-Slip & Liquid-Friction Ways

Definition. Stick-slip = jerky slow-feed motion because static friction > kinetic friction: the member sticks until drive force exceeds static friction, then slips suddenly. Cures: hydrodynamic/hydrostatic/anti-friction ways, anti-stick-slip oils, PTFE facing.

Hydrodynamic: wedge film lifts only at high speed (used where motion is the primary cutting motion). Hydrostatic: pumped pocket gives film at any speed → high load at zero speed, no starting friction, negligible wear, high stiffness, good damping; but costly and hard to lock. Multiple pockets need a restrictor (capillary lc≥20dc, or orifice) per pocket so one pump serves all; max stiffness at p₀/pp = 2/3. Film gap varies as load^(1/3) (vs ^(1/2) hydrodynamic → hydrostatic far stiffer).

6. Aerostatic & Anti-friction Ways

Aerostatic (air film): no oil, clamp by cutting air, self-cleaning, tiny pull force (~1 kgf); but low stiffness (~10 kgf/µm) & damping → setting motions of precision machines only. Anti-friction (rolling): low friction, no stick-slip, high stiffness if preloaded, high speed; but low load per element (point/line contact), costly, dirt-sensitive. The element centre moves at half the carriage speed → either limit travel or recirculate balls/rollers.

7. Power Screws & Ball Screws

Sliding power screws use the Acme (trapezoidal) thread; design criteria: (1) wear/bearing pressure, (2) strength (core under axial+torsion), (3) buckling (column), (4) pitch accuracy (lead screw). The recirculating ball screw rolls hardened balls in helical grooves:

TQ — Stick-slip CLO3

Q: Explain stick-slip in slideways, why it occurs, and how it is overcome. (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

At slow feed the member moves in jerks because static μ > kinetic μ: the drive builds force to beat static friction, the member slips, friction drops, stored energy releases → jerk, then sticks again (worsened by μ falling as oil is squeezed out over time). It ruins slow-feed smoothness and positioning. Cures: hydrodynamic/hydrostatic (zero starting friction) or anti-friction ways, anti-stick-slip oils, PTFE facing.

TQ — Why a restrictor in hydrostatic ways CLO3

Q: Why can't several hydrostatic pockets be fed directly from one pump, and how does a restrictor solve it? (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

Pockets carry different loads → need different pressures. Fed directly, the lower-loaded pocket lifts first and all excess oil escapes through it, so the heavier pocket never reaches its required pressure. A restrictor (capillary lc≥20dc, or orifice) in each branch passes only limited flow, letting one pump hold a high supply pressure pp while each pocket builds its own p₀; max stiffness at p₀/pp = 2/3.

TQ — Anti-friction ways & recirculation CLO3

Q: State advantages/limitations of anti-friction guideways and why recirculation is needed. (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

Adv: low friction, uniform slow-speed motion (no stick-slip), high stiffness when preloaded, high speed. Limits: low load per element (point/line contact, needs hard ways), costlier, dirt-sensitive. Recirculation: the rolling element's centre moves at half the carriage velocity, so elements lag and run out over a long stroke; recirculating them keeps enough between the surfaces for unlimited travel.

TQ — Ball screw vs Acme screw CLO3

Q: What is a recirculating ball screw? Advantages over an Acme screw and why preferred on NC/CNC? (5 marks)

Show answer ▸

Hardened balls roll in matching helical grooves of screw and nut and recirculate through a return tube. Adv: rolling friction → ~90% efficiency (vs 30–50%), no stick-slip, backlash eliminated by preloaded double nut → high stiffness, long life. NC/CNC: high efficiency + zero backlash + high stiffness → accurate repeatable servo positioning. Trade-off: costly, not self-locking (needs a brake on vertical axes).

CLO5 (Module 6) — Vibrations in Machine Tools

Mehta ch 6 · course PPT Module 6. Identify sources of vibration and the means to eliminate it. (Mostly theory — high yield for the 60% theory block.)

Definition. Vibration in machine tools = unwanted oscillatory motion of components, tool, workpiece or structure during machining, arising whenever an exciting force or process instability disturbs the steady tool–work motion. Even a tiny tool-tip vibration prints a visible mark on the surface.

1. Sources of Vibration

  • Misalignment of shafts/couplings → periodic forces, noise, heat, bearing wear.
  • Gear errors — profile/pitch error, eccentricity, backlash, worn teeth → dynamic forces at meshing (forced excitation).
  • Bearing defects — wear, damaged elements, poor lubrication, wrong preload/clearance → corrupt spindle accuracy.
  • Cutting-force variation — variable chip thickness, tool wear, interrupted cut, BUE, uneven feed/depth (the process exciting force).
  • Tool wear/geometry — blunt tool, bad rake/clearance, long overhang, loose clamping.
  • Workpiece flexibility — slender/thin parts deflect & vibrate.
  • Structural flexibility of bed/column/housing; external disturbances (floor, nearby machines, weak foundation).
Exam point. Mehta frames this as a closed loop: the elastic system (ES) and the cutting process feed back — cutting force deflects the structure → deflection changes chip thickness → chip thickness changes the force. Whether a disturbance grows into chatter depends on the dynamic stability of this loop.

3. Resonance

Definition. Resonance = forcing frequency ≈ a natural frequency (r ≈ 1). The magnification peaks (Mmax ≈ 1/2ζ), amplitude becomes very large (limited only by damping). Avoid by changing spindle speed or shifting ωn via mass/stiffness (ωn = √(k/m)).

4. Self-Excited Vibration & Chatter

Self-excited (chatter) is generated and sustained by the cutting process itself — no external periodic force; energy comes from the cut (mode coupling). Chatter frequency ≈ a structural natural frequency.

  • Primary chatter — instability in the cut itself (friction, chip-formation instability, BUE, mode coupling).
  • Regenerative chatter — the dominant type: waviness left by the previous revolution/tooth pass modulates the chip thickness on the current cut; the phase shift makes chip thickness — and force — fluctuate, amplifying the vibration.
Exam point — limiting width of cut. Regenerative stability is set by a critical width blim: above it chatter grows. Larger dynamic stiffness (↑k, ↑damping) → larger blim. Changing spindle speed is the most effective fix — it shifts the phase between successive waves (stability lobes), not just the amplitude.

6. Vibration Control

  1. Reduce the source (most effective): balance rotating parts, align shafts, replace bearings, improve gears, reduce backlash, tighten parts, improve foundation.
  2. Increase stiffness: box/ribbed structures, thicker sections, short overhang, rigid holders, support long work (↑k raises ωn & blim).
  3. Increase damping: cast-iron / polymer-concrete beds, damping layers, viscous/friction/constrained-layer damping, tuned mass dampers.
  4. Avoid resonance: change spindle speed or mass/stiffness.
  5. Cutting conditions / tool geometry: tune speed/feed/depth; sharp tool, correct rake/clearance, optimum nose radius, minimal overhang, rigid holder.
  6. Workpiece support & clamping: tailstock, steady/follower rest, fixtures, firm clamping.
  7. Foundation & isolators: heavy blocks, anti-vibration mounts (rubber/spring/cork/pneumatic — but too-soft reduces stability).
  8. Dampers & chatter control: tuned mass / dynamic absorbers; variable-pitch cutters; for regenerative chatter change spindle speed.

Q: Describe regenerative chatter and contrast with primary chatter. (5 marks)
