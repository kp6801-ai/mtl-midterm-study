CLO1

Drives, Process Kinematics & Working Motions

Q1.[3]

Define generatrix and directrix. List the four methods of generating a surface and give one machine-tool example of each.

Model answer
Generatrix = the line/profile that produces the surface; directrix = the path along which the generatrix is moved. The actual machined surface is the trace of generatrix swept along directrix.
  • Tracing (forming by feed): turning a cylinder — generatrix = tool edge, directrix = circle from rotation.
  • Forming: form tool reproduces its own profile (form turning, gear by form cutter).
  • Copying: stylus follows a template (copy-turning, profile milling).
  • Generation: rolling/enveloping motion (gear hobbing, gear shaping).
Q2.[3]

Distinguish primary cutting motion, feed motion and auxiliary motion. State the working motions in turning, drilling and milling.

Model answer
  • Primary (cutting) motion — the high-speed motion that removes the chip.
  • Feed motion — slow motion that brings fresh material into the cut, sustains removal.
  • Auxiliary motion — setting, indexing, rapid traverse, clamping — no cutting.
Turning: primary = workpiece rotation, feed = tool linear travel. Drilling: primary = drill rotation, feed = drill axial. Milling: primary = cutter rotation, feed = table travel.
Q3.[4]

With a block diagram, describe the kinematic chain of an engine lathe from motor to spindle and to the carriage feed. What does a kinematic structure diagram show?

Model answer
Motor → belt → speed gearbox (headstock) → spindle (speed chain). Spindle → tumbler/change gears → feed gearbox → lead screw / feed rod → apron → carriage (feed chain). Thread cutting engages the lead screw via the half-nut. A kinematic structure diagram shows every shaft, gear, and transmission ratio symbolically — the path of motion and the available speed/feed steps, independent of the physical layout.
CLO2

Regulation of Speed & Feed

Q4.[4]

Why are spindle speeds arranged in geometric progression rather than arithmetic? Define the step ratio φ and list the standard preferred values.

Model answer
GP keeps the relative speed loss constant across the whole range, so the maximum percentage drop of cutting speed between two adjacent steps is the same at every diameter — economical speed selection at all sizes. Arithmetic spacing wastes the high-speed end. Step ratio φ = nₖ₊₁/nₖ = constant. Standard φ: 1.06, 1.12, 1.26, 1.41, 1.58, 1.78, 2.0 (derived from the R20/R40 preferred-number series).
Q5.[3]

Differentiate stepped and stepless (infinitely variable) drives; give one example of each and one merit/demerit.

Model answer
Stepped: discrete speeds via gearboxes (sliding-gear / cone-pulley). Merit: positive drive, holds torque; demerit: only fixed speeds, cannot hit exact optimum V. Stepless: continuous speed (PIV/variable-pulley, hydraulic, DC/AC variable-speed motor). Merit: exact cutting speed at any diameter; demerit: slip / lower stiffness / cost.
Q6.[3]

A gearbox gives z speeds from nmin to nmax. Write φ and the range ratio Rn. What is a ray (speed) diagram used for?

Model answer
φ = (nmax/nmin)1/(z−1); range ratio Rn = nmax/nmin = φ(z−1). A ray/structural diagram plots spindle speeds (log scale) against shafts, each transmission shown as a sloped ray — it lays out how many speeds, the ratios on each shaft, and checks that no ratio violates the 1/4 ≤ i ≤ 2 limit.
CLO3

Guideways — Functions & Requirements

Q7.[3]

State the principal functions of a machine-tool guideway.

Model answer
  • Provide accurate guided motion along a straight/defined path.
  • Carry loads — cutting force, weight of moving member, dynamic loads.
  • Maintain alignment — preserve the geometric tool–workpiece relationship.
Q8.[4]

List the requirements of a good guideway and classify guideways by contact type with one advantage of each.

Model answer
Requirements: geometric accuracy of motion, high stiffness/rigidity, wear resistance & constant accuracy over life, good damping, low friction (no stick-slip), ease of manufacture & adjustment.
  • Sliding — simple, high damping, cheap.
  • Rolling — very low friction, no stick-slip, high positioning accuracy.
  • Hydrostatic — oil film, near-zero wear & friction, excellent damping.
  • Aerostatic — air film, frictionless, for ultra-precision/light loads.
Q9.[3]

Explain the stick-slip phenomenon — its cause, effect, and two remedies.

Model answer
Cause: static friction > kinetic friction, so at low feed the slide alternately sticks (elastic wind-up) then jumps (slip) → jerky motion, position error, poor finish. Remedies: anti-friction (PTFE/Turcite) facings or rolling/hydrostatic ways to make μk ≥ μs; special anti-stick-slip lubricants; increase drive stiffness.
Q10.[3]

Compare flat, vee and dovetail guideways. Why does the dovetail prevent lifting of the moving member?

Model answer
Flat — easy to make, high load area, but needs a strip/gib to retain & no self-alignment. Vee — self-aligning, wear automatically compensated, but lower load capacity. Dovetail — compact, resists overturning and lift-off because the inclined faces wrap the mating member; used on tool slides/cross-slides. The inclined retaining faces take the tipping/lift force, so the slide cannot rise off the way.
CLO4

Strength & Stiffness of Structures

Q11.[3]

A bed under torsion is built as a closed box, but a member under bending as an I-section. Justify with the relevant section property.

Model answer
Torsional rigidity of a thin closed tube ∝ enclosed mid-line area (Bredt: J ≈ 4A₀²δ/perimeter) — a closed box gives a huge A₀, far stiffer in torsion than any open section. For bending, stiffness ∝ I about the bending axis; an I-section puts material in the flanges far from the neutral axis → maximum I per unit weight. Hence box for torsion, I for bending.
Q12.[3]

Why is stiffness, not strength, the governing criterion in machine-tool structure and spindle design?

Model answer
Machining accuracy depends on elastic deflection under cutting load, not on failure. Deflection limits are reached long before stress limits. For spindles, all steels have nearly the same E, so a stronger alloy gives no stiffness gain — material/section is chosen for rigidity, wear and accuracy, and design is to a permissible deflection [y].
Q13.[3]

How are bases and tables analysed for stiffness? What is reduced (bending) stiffness and how do stiffeners affect it?

Model answer
A base/table is treated as a plate on an elastic foundation; the maximum slope/deflection from the distributed loads and applied moments must stay within the permissible angle of slope. Reduced stiffness EIbr = s·E·Iwall accounts for the walls plus stiffeners. Closely-spaced perpendicular stiffeners recover most of the solid-box rigidity; diagonal stiffeners are even better for combined bending+torsion.
Q14.[4]

Define machine-tool compliance and explain how the compliances of saddle, headstock and tailstock combine to affect machining accuracy. State the effect of bearing preload on spindle stiffness.

Model answer
Compliance = deflection per unit force (1/stiffness). The cutting force displaces headstock centre, tailstock centre and the saddle/tool; the displacements combine (position-weighted) to give the tool-point error → diameter/taper error. System compliance = machine-tool + workpiece bending in series. Preload removes bearing clearance (no sudden jump on load reversal) and shifts the support boundary condition from simply-supported toward clamped → stiffer spindle, less nose deflection. Excess preload → heat & shorter bearing life, so it must be optimum.
CLO5

Vibration — Sources & Elimination

Q15.[3]

Classify machine-tool vibrations and give the source of each.

Model answer
  • Free/transient — from a sudden shock (start of cut, impact); decays with damping.
  • Forced — a periodic exciting force: rotor unbalance, gear/bearing errors, intermittent (interrupted) cut, drive pulsation.
  • Self-excited (chatter) — energy fed from the cutting process itself; grows without external periodic force.
Q16.[3]

Explain regenerative chatter.

Model answer
Each pass leaves a wavy surface; the next overlapping cut meets that waviness, so chip thickness varies and the cutting force oscillates. A phase lag between the inner and outer modulation feeds energy into the structure → self-sustaining, growing vibration. Governed by the stability lobe diagram (depth of cut vs spindle speed).
Q17.[4]

List the means to suppress / eliminate machine-tool vibration.

Model answer
  • Raise static & dynamic stiffness (shorter overhang, bigger sections, preload).
  • Add damping — filled/ribbed castings, dampers, polymer-concrete beds.
  • Dynamic vibration absorber / tuned mass damper on the tool or spindle.
  • Vibration isolation — anti-vibration mounts to cut foundation transmission.
  • Change process parameters — speed (to a stable lobe), feed, depth, tool geometry (variable pitch / helix).
