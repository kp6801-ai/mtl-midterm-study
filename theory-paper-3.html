CLO1

Drives, Process Kinematics & Working Motions

Q1.[4]

Compare the forming and generation methods of producing a surface. Which gives higher accuracy for gears and why?

Model answer
Forming — the tool profile is the exact mirror of the surface; one form cut reproduces it (form-cutter gear). Simple, fast, but accuracy depends entirely on tool-profile accuracy and a separate cutter is needed per profile/size. Generation — the surface is enveloped by the relative rolling motion of tool and work (hobbing/gear shaping); one cutter makes many sizes and the profile is mathematically correct. Generation gives higher gear accuracy because the tooth form is created by the conjugate rolling action, not copied from a tool.

Q3.[3]

Compare arithmetic and geometric progression of spindle speeds for machine-tool drives.

Model answer
AP: constant difference between speeds — relative loss is large at low rpm and tiny at high rpm, so the high range is wasted and many steps are needed. GP: constant ratio — equal percentage speed loss at every step, economical at all diameters, fewest steps for a given range. Machine tools therefore always use GP (standardised φ).
CLO3

Guideways

Q4.[4]

Compare flat, vee and dovetail guideways. Why does the dovetail resist lift-off?

Model answer
Flat — easy to make, large load-bearing area, but needs a gib/strip to retain and gives no self-alignment. Vee — self-aligning and wear is automatically compensated by gravity, but lower load capacity and harder to make. Dovetail — compact, takes loads in several directions and resists overturning. Its inclined retaining faces wrap the mating member, so any lifting/tipping force is reacted by those faces — the slide cannot rise off the way (used on tool/cross-slides).

Q6.[3]

Of the flat-flat, flat-V and V-V slideway combinations, which gives the least radial deflection? Justify briefly.

Model answer
V-V. The symmetric V faces split the load evenly and produce no net overturning couple, so the carriage stays centred and radial deflection is least. Flat-flat needs gibs and allows more lateral play; flat-V is intermediate. (Proved by comparing the reaction-induced deflections of each combination.)
CLO4

Strength & Stiffness of Structures

Q7.[3]

Compare perpendicular and diagonal stiffeners for stiffening a bed/table wall.

Model answer
Perpendicular stiffeners (ribs at right angles to the walls) recover most of the bending rigidity of a solid box and are easy to cast. Diagonal stiffeners additionally form triangulated frames, so they resist torsion and combined bending+torsion better and give higher rigidity for the same weight — preferred where twisting is significant, at some pattern/casting complexity.

Q9.[3]

A designer proposes a higher-strength alloy steel to make a spindle stiffer. Evaluate this choice.

Model answer
It will not help. Spindle deflection depends on the modulus of elasticity E and the section, not on strength; all steels have nearly the same E (≈2.1×10⁶ kgf/cm²), so a stronger alloy gives essentially no stiffness gain. To raise stiffness you increase the section/diameter, shorten the overhang, or improve bearing support/preload. The better alloy is justified only for wear/hardness at the nose, not for rigidity.

Q11.[3]

Compare primary chatter and regenerative chatter.

Model answer
Primary chatter arises from instabilities in the cutting process itself (friction, built-up edge, chip-formation/force variation) without needing a previous surface. Regenerative chatter (the more common and more restrictive) arises from cutting the wavy surface left by the previous pass — the regenerative phase feeds energy in; its limiting depth of cut is roughly half that for primary chatter, so it is harder to avoid.
Q12.[4]

Compare passive and active vibration control. When is each appropriate?

Model answer
Passive — inherent damping, isolators, tuned-mass absorbers; no power or sensing, simple, reliable, fixed tuning. Best for known, steady disturbances and most production machines. Active — sensor → controller → actuator feedback applies a counter-force in real time; adapts to changing conditions and targets specific modes, but needs power, instrumentation and is costly. Reserved for advanced precision/high-value machine tools where passive means are insufficient.
