Vibration — diagnose & fix

Q1.[5]

A slender shaft turned between centres shows chatter marks and a wavy surface worst near mid-span. Diagnose the problem and recommend remedies.

Model answer
Diagnosis: low workpiece stiffness at mid-span → regenerative chatter (waviness worst where deflection is largest). Remedies: support the work with a steady/follower rest at mid-span; reduce tool overhang and use a rigid holder; lower the depth of cut; change the spindle speed to a stable lobe; use a sharp tool with correct nose radius; increase tailstock support/preload. Result: higher dynamic stiffness and a chatter-free cut.
Q2.[4]

Face-milling a thin plate gives poor flatness, tooth chipping and loud noise. Identify the causes and the control methods.

Model answer
Causes: interrupted cutting (teeth entering/leaving) gives fluctuating force; low workpiece/fixture stiffness; possibly worn cutter or excessive depth. Control: clamp the plate firmly with support under the cut; reduce axial/radial depth; use a variable-pitch cutter and sharp inserts; rigid short cutter holder; choose a suitable spindle speed and the correct milling direction (climb vs conventional).
Q3.[3]

A deep drill wanders off-centre and vibrates. Recommend fixes.

Model answer
Use the shortest drill possible (long drills are flexible); start with a pilot/centre hole for guidance; correct point angle and proper feed/speed; peck-drill with coolant for chip clearance; check spindle run-out and drill alignment; ensure firm workpiece support. These raise stiffness and guidance, stopping wander and vibration.

Guideway & spindle selection

Q4.[4]

Recommend a guideway type for a heavy precision jig-boring machine and justify the choice.

Model answer
Choose hydrostatic guideways: an oil film under pressure gives near-zero friction and wear (no stick-slip), very high stiffness and excellent damping, holding accuracy over a long life under heavy load — exactly what a precision jig borer needs. (Rolling ways are an alternative for lower loads; plain sliding ways risk stick-slip and wear.)
Q5.[4]

A spindle nose shows excessive radial run-out that jumps when the cutting load reverses. State the likely cause and the remedy.

Model answer
Likely cause: bearing clearance/play — with clearance the spindle shifts before the rolling elements take load, and the deflection jumps on load reversal, worsened if the bearing span is short (λ<2.5). Remedy: preload the bearings (interference fit) to remove play and stiffen the support (toward a clamped condition); ensure λ ≥ 2.5; check/replace worn bearings. Keep preload optimum to avoid overheating.
Q6.[4]

For a high-speed precision lathe spindle, recommend a bearing arrangement at the front support and justify it.

Model answer
Use angular-contact ball bearings at the front support (preloaded, in a back-to-back set): they give low heat generation and fair axial+radial stiffness, suiting high rotational speed and good run-out. Avoid only-cylindrical-roller front sets (raise axial thermal growth at the nose) and taper-roller sets (raise run-out). For very high speed/accuracy, hydrodynamic/hydrostatic sleeve bearings are the alternative (high damping).

Process & drive decisions

Q7.[4]

Select suitable cutting speed and feed for (a) rough turning of mild steel and (b) finish turning of aluminium, and justify the difference.

Model answer
(a) Rough mild steel: moderate speed, high feed, large depth — maximise removal; steel needs a moderate V to protect tool life. (b) Finish aluminium: high speed, low feed, small depth — aluminium tolerates high V, and a small feed gives a fine finish and accuracy. General rule: rough = high feed/depth for MRR; finish = low feed/high speed for finish; harder material → lower speed.
Q8.[3]

For drilling a through-hole, identify the primary, feed and any auxiliary motions and the surface-generation method.

Model answer
Primary = drill rotation (cutting); feed = drill axial advance; auxiliary = table positioning, rapid approach/retract, clamping. The cylindrical hole is generated by a multi-point tool: the rotating cutting lips (generatrix) swept along the axial feed path (directrix).
Q9.[3]

A workshop runs several old machines from one motor through a line shaft. Comment on this drive choice and give a modern alternative.

Model answer
That is a group (line-shaft) drive — cheap historically but inefficient, noisy, unsafe, with no independent speed control and total stoppage if the shaft/belt fails. Modern alternative: an individual drive per machine (own motor, often a variable-frequency drive) — independent speeds, higher efficiency, better control and safety.

Chatter control & troubleshooting

Q10.[4]

Chatter appears at one spindle speed. Explain how merely changing the spindle speed can stop it.

Model answer
Regenerative chatter depends on the phase between the wave left last pass and the wave being cut now, set by the time per revolution τ. Changing the spindle speed changes τ and hence the phase, moving the operating point from an unstable into a stable lobe of the stability-lobe diagram — so the same depth of cut becomes chatter-free. (Picking a speed under a lobe peak even allows a deeper stable cut.)
Q11.[4]

A machine suddenly becomes noisy and vibrates. Give a practical troubleshooting sequence.

Model answer
Listen/measure to localise the frequency; check the obvious sources first — tool sharpness, overhang and clamping, workpiece support, then unbalance, bearing condition, gear/belt errors, alignment, foundation bolts. Try a speed/feed/depth change to test for chatter; inspect bearings and preload; tighten loose fasteners. Fix the source first, then add stiffness/damping if vibration persists.
