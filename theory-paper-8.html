Machine-Tool Dynamics

Q1.[6]

Describe the machine tool as a closed-loop system (elastic system + cutting process) and state how the Nyquist criterion is used to judge dynamic stability.

Model answer
The machine-tool elastic system (MTES) and the cutting process (CP) form a closed loop: a relative tool–work displacement changes the chip thickness → changes the cutting force → deflects the structure → changes the displacement. Stability is assessed from the open-loop frequency response (polar/Nyquist plot). By the Nyquist criterion, the system is stable if the open-loop locus crosses the negative-real axis to the right of (−1, 0) and unstable if it crosses to the left; the crossing point fixes the limiting depth of cut and chatter frequency.
Q2.[6]

Compare the Tlusty (static) and Kudinov/Tobias (time-lag) models of the cutting process. Explain phase lag vs phase lead and the damping contributed by the tool flank wear land.

Model answer
Tlusty: dynamic force proportional to and instantaneous with chip-thickness change (P = ksy) — a static, frequency-independent, real characteristic (no phase). Kudinov/Tobias: the force builds up over a chip-formation time constant Tc, so W = ks/(Tcp+1) — the force lags the displacement, which pumps energy in and aggravates vibration. A force component that leads the displacement instead removes energy (damping). The rubbing of the flank wear land produces such a leading component — process damping — so a small wear land (h≈0.1–0.2 mm) actually improves stability.
Q3.[4]

Define dynamic stiffness and receptance, and explain how they differ from static stiffness.

Model answer
Static stiffness = force/deflection under a steady load (a constant). Dynamic stiffness = the frequency-dependent ratio of a harmonic force to the resulting harmonic displacement; it is complex (real = stiffness, imaginary = damping) and drops sharply near resonance. Receptance = its inverse, displacement per unit force (a frequency-response function). Chatter resistance depends on the minimum dynamic stiffness (peak receptance), not the static value.

Speed Regulation — Theory

Q4.[5]

Show how the standard values of the step ratio φ arise from (i) the two-speed-motor condition and (ii) the preferred-number requirement.

Model answer
(i) Drives often use two-speed motors whose speeds are in the ratio 2, so after some u steps of the GP the speed must double: φu = 2. (ii) The series should also fit the preferred numbers (R-series), where speeds repeat every decade: φE = 10. Both must hold for integer step counts, which forces φ to the standard set 1.06, 1.12, 1.26, 1.41, 1.58, 1.78, 2.0 (e.g. 1.06 = 101/40 = 21/12). These are the only ratios that simultaneously double and decade-repeat at whole numbers of steps.
Q5.[4]

State the economic aim of speed/feed regulation: give the machining-cost components and the idea of optimum cutting speed.

Model answer
Aim: choose v and feed to minimise cost per component. Cost = machining-time cost + non-productive cost + tool-changing cost + tool cost: C = (W+E)tm + (W+E)tnp + (W+E)tc(tm/T) + Ct(tm/T). As speed rises, machining time falls but tool life T falls (Taylor vTⁿ=C), raising tool/changing cost — so total cost has a minimum at an optimum cutting speed vopt (and similarly sopt). The regulated drive must make vopt available.
Q6.[4]

Explain the harmonic and logarithmic laws of stepped regulation and why neither is used in practice.

Model answer
Harmonic keeps the diameter range served by each speed constant: 1/nk − 1/nk+1 = constant. This packs steps sensibly only at large diameters and gives an awkward, very wide rpm spread at small diameters. Logarithmic is a limiting/special case with similar drawbacks. Neither gives the constant relative (percentage) speed loss that GP gives, so both are impractical — GP is adopted.

Fluid-Film Bearings & Ways

Q7.[6]

Explain the working principle of hydrodynamic, hydrostatic and aerostatic bearings used as spindle/slideway supports — how the supporting film is generated in each.

Model answer
Hydrodynamic: relative motion drags oil into a converging wedge, generating pressure that lifts and separates the surfaces — load capacity exists only when moving (no film at rest/low speed). Hydrostatic: oil is pumped in under external pressure through restrictors to pockets, so a stiff film carries load even at zero speed — very high stiffness, no wear, excellent damping. Aerostatic: same idea but with pressurised air — frictionless and clean for ultra-precision/light loads, but low stiffness/damping and risk of instability.
Q8.[4]

In a hydrostatic pad bearing, explain how a restrictor (capillary or orifice) controls the load capacity and stiffness.

Model answer
Each pocket is fed from a constant-pressure supply through a restrictor. When load increases, the gap narrows and pocket outflow drops, so the pressure drop across the restrictor falls and pocket pressure rises automatically to support the extra load — giving stiffness. A capillary restrictor gives flow ∝ pressure drop (linear); an orifice gives flow ∝ √(pressure drop) (higher stiffness, more sensitive). Sizing the restrictor sets the pad's stiffness and flow.

Spindle Support Modelling

Q9.[4]

When a spindle runs in a sleeve (journal) bearing, how is that support represented in the design diagram, and what is the reactive moment Mr?

Model answer
A sleeve bearing resists not just a transverse force but also a slight tilt, so it is modelled as a simple hinge plus a reactive moment Mr acting at the middle of the bearing (a ball/anti-friction bearing is modelled as a plain hinge only). Mr = k·M, where M is the bending moment at the support and k ≈ 0–0.35 (k→0 at light load, up to ≈0.3–0.35 at heavy load). Including Mr makes the support stiffer than a pure hinge, reducing computed nose deflection.
CLO1

Surface Generation

Q10.[4]

Distinguish single-point and multi-point generation of surfaces, giving the tool–work kinematics of each.

Model answer
Single-point: one cutting edge produces the surface by combined primary + feed motion — turning, shaping, planing (generatrix = the single edge, swept along the directrix). Multi-point: many edges act together — milling, drilling, broaching, grinding — each edge removing part of the layer; the envelope of all edges (with the feed) forms the surface. Multi-point gives higher MRR and (for milling/grinding) better finish, but interrupted cutting raises dynamic force.
