CLO1

Drives, Process Kinematics & Working Motions

Q1.[4]

Explain, using the generatrix–directrix idea, how a cylindrical surface is produced in turning and a flat surface in shaping.

Model answer
Turning: the generatrix is a straight line parallel to the axis (formed by the tool feeding along the bed); the directrix is the circle produced by the rotating workpiece. Sweeping the line around the circle generates the cylinder. Shaping: the generatrix is the straight line cut by the tool's stroke; the directrix is the straight intermittent feed of the table — line swept along a line gives a flat surface.
Q2.[5]

With a block diagram, explain the kinematic chain of an engine lathe from motor to spindle and to the carriage feed. What does a kinematic structure diagram convey?

Model answer
Speed chain: motor → belt → speed gearbox (headstock) → spindle. Feed chain: spindle → tumbler/change gears → feed gearbox → lead screw (for threading) or feed rod → apron → carriage/cross-slide. The half-nut engages the lead screw for thread cutting. A kinematic structure diagram represents every shaft, gear and transmission ratio symbolically — it shows the path of motion and the full set of available speeds/feeds, independent of the physical layout.
CLO2

Regulation of Speed & Feed

Q3.[4]

Explain why a geometric progression of spindle speeds is preferred over an arithmetic one. Use the idea of relative speed loss.

Model answer
For a fixed cutting speed V, the rpm needed varies inversely with diameter. With a GP, the ratio between adjacent speeds is constant, so the maximum percentage loss of cutting speed when you must drop to the next available step is the same at every diameter — uniformly economical. With an AP the steps are too coarse at low rpm and needlessly fine at high rpm, wasting the high-speed range. GP therefore gives equal relative economy and the fewest steps for a given range.
Q4.[4]

Explain what a ray (structural) diagram and a speed diagram show, and how they help design a speed gearbox.

Model answer
The structural (ray) diagram plots the shafts as vertical lines and each transmission as a sloped ray, showing the number of speeds and the ratio steps on each shaft (the "structure" e.g. 3×2). The speed diagram places the actual spindle speeds on a logarithmic scale (so GP steps are equal spacings) and the ray slopes give the gear ratios. Together they let the designer fix the number of stages, distribute the ratios, and check that every ratio stays within ¼ ≤ i ≤ 2.
CLO3

Guideways

Q5.[4]

Explain the stick-slip phenomenon — its cause, its effect on machining, and two ways to eliminate it.

Model answer
Cause: static friction exceeds kinetic friction, so at low feed the slide elastically "winds up" until the drive force overcomes static friction, then suddenly "slips," repeatedly — jerky intermittent motion. Effect: positioning error, poor surface finish, loss of fine-feed accuracy. Remedies: use anti-friction facings (PTFE/Turcite) or rolling/hydrostatic ways so μkinetic ≥ μstatic; use anti-stick-slip lubricants; increase the drive stiffness.
Q6.[4]

Explain how the reactions on a combination of V and flat slideways are found, and how the design then checks wear resistance.

Model answer
Draw the free body of the carriage with cutting forces (Pz, Py) and weight G. Write three equilibrium equations — ΣY, ΣZ and ΣM about the axis — to solve for the V-face reactions A, B and the flat-face reaction C. The largest reaction divided by its bearing area gives the maximum pressure, which must satisfy p ≤ [p] for wear resistance; the deflection check then covers stiffness.
CLO4

Strength & Stiffness of Structures

Q7.[4]

Explain why a bed loaded mainly in torsion is built as a closed box, while a member loaded in bending is built as an I-section.

Model answer
Torsional rigidity of a thin closed tube ∝ the enclosed mid-line area (Bredt: J ≈ 4A₀²δ/perimeter); a closed box encloses a large area and resists the shear flow all round, so it is far stiffer in torsion than any open section. Bending stiffness ∝ I about the bending axis; an I-section concentrates material in the flanges, far from the neutral axis, maximising I per unit weight. Hence box for torsion, I for bending.
Q8.[4]

Explain how the compliance of the machine tool affects machining accuracy when turning between centres.

Model answer
Under the radial force the headstock centre, tailstock centre and the saddle/tool each deflect by force×compliance. Their deflections combine (position-weighted along the work) to displace the tool relative to the workpiece, producing a diameter error that varies along the length → taper/profile error. The "system" compliance also adds the workpiece's own bending in series. Lower, more balanced compliances (equal-stiffness centres) minimise and even out the error.
Q9.[3]

Explain how preloading the anti-friction bearings increases spindle stiffness, and why excessive preload is harmful.

Model answer
Preload removes the bearing clearance so there is no sudden jump in deflection when the load reverses, and it stiffens the contact — the support behaves less like a simple hinge and more like a clamp, shifting the spindle toward a clamped beam (stiffer, smaller nose deflection). Too much preload raises contact stress and heat generation, accelerating wear and shortening bearing life — so an optimum preload is used.
CLO5

Vibration

Q10.[4]

Explain the mechanism of regenerative chatter.

Model answer
The first pass leaves a wavy surface. On the next revolution (or next tooth) the tool cuts that waviness while also vibrating, so the instantaneous chip thickness = current displacement minus the (overlap-factor scaled) wave left previously: δ = y(t) − m·y(t−τ). A phase lag between the outer (previous) and inner (current) waves makes the cutting force feed energy into the structure, so the vibration grows — self-excited. Whether it grows depends on spindle speed (which sets the phase τ); governed by the stability lobe diagram.
Q11.[4]

Explain what a stability lobe diagram is and how it is used to choose cutting conditions.

Model answer
It plots the limiting (chatter-free) axial depth of cut against spindle speed. Below the lowest point (blim,min) the cut is stable at any speed. Above it, alternating "lobes" of stable and unstable speeds appear because changing speed changes the regenerative phase. The machinist picks a spindle speed sitting under a lobe peak to allow a larger stable depth of cut — getting higher material-removal rate without chatter.
Q12.[3]

Explain how damping is introduced into machine-tool structures and why it matters.

Model answer
Damping dissipates vibrational energy, limiting amplitude at resonance and helping stability. It is obtained from the high internal damping of cast-iron / polymer-concrete beds, from filled/ribbed castings, from friction at bolted joints (joint damping), from constrained-layer damping treatments, and from added viscous dampers or tuned-mass absorbers. Higher damping → lower vibration amplitude and a higher chatter-free depth of cut.
